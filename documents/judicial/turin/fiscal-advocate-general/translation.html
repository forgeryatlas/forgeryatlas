Copy of a note from the Fiscal Advocate General at the Court of Appeal of Turin, dated January 21, 1859.

In the first days of February 1858, the Public Security authorities discovered, on the outskirts of this capital, near the Madonna del Pilone, and in several rooms rented by certain individuals, Varallo Pandolfini, Louis Venanzi Alessandro, Spadafora Filippo, and Mayner Vieller Augustin, a workshop where counterfeit Turkish paper money was being manufactured. In this activity there had also taken part a certain person named Calvocoressi Dimitri, who at that time had gone to Constantinople to circulate the counterfeit paper and where he had been arrested. Several counterfeit caimes, machines, and tools used for the said manufacture were seized, and Venanzi and Varallo Pandolfini were arrested; Spadafora and Mayner Vieller having succeeded in escaping.

The four individuals mentioned above were indicted by judgment of May 1, 1858, and their case will be brought to trial before the Court of Appeal of this city at the hearing of February 28 next.

The charge against them is as follows:
“Manufacture of foreign paper money, for having, during the last months of 1857 and the beginning of 1858, in Turin, in a house located at the Madonna del Pilone, acted in concert among themselves in the production, to the detriment of the Sublime Porte, of public credit paper of that Empire issued under the name of caimes, or banknotes of the Ottoman Government of the value of twenty piastres each; having produced up to 17,701 notes corresponding to 354,020 piastres, a large part of which was allegedly exported from the Royal States. Crime provided for by Article 347 of the Penal Code and punishable by a criminal penalty.”

Hardly had the arrest of Venanzi and Varallo Pandolfini and the seizure of the counterfeit caimes and the objects used in their manufacture taken place when the Representative of the Sublime Porte, in the name of his government, constituted himself as a civil party by an act dated March 2, 1858, and made a deposit of 300 francs in accordance with Article 94 of the Code of Criminal Procedure.

When the investigation of this case was nearing its end, it came to the attention of the Ministry of the Interior and the police that one Ambroise Bondesio, who had been living for several months in Turin after having long resided in Constantinople, had worked both in Constantinople and in Turin in the manufacture of counterfeit Turkish paper money and might also be involved in the aforementioned case. Varallo Pandolfini, when questioned on this matter, stated that he had learned from Mayner Vieller and a certain Angelo Gennari that, in Bondesio’s own house in Constantinople, in a room on the third floor, Turkish paper money was being manufactured jointly by Angelo Gennari, Mayner Vieller, and Venanzi; that Bondesio was not unaware of this activity, that he profited from it, and that within the Bondesio household this counterfeit paper money was used for daily expenses. On the basis of these indications, Mr. Bondesio was arrested on April 8, 1858.

The first task of the investigating judge was to establish that Bondesio had taken part, together with Venanzi, Varallo Pandolfini, Spadafora, and Mayner, in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Turin. All the inquiries and investigations were negative. The same Varallo Pandolfini, who identified Bondesio as having participated in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Constantinople, not only did not implicate him, but excluded him from participation in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Turin; likewise, the other detainee, Alessandro Venanzi, also excluded him in his statements on this matter.

However, several reports from the Turkish authorities (transmitted to the judge by the Minister of Justice in a note dated July 10 last) were received, according to which these authorities appeared to be convinced that the group of counterfeiters established in Constantinople in Bondesio’s house had moved to Turin, where Bondesio himself had also gone, and that he even had another associate in Genoa with whom he continued to manufacture counterfeit caimes.

These reports were merely opinions, reasonings, and inferences that had no sufficient support, and were in fact entirely contradicted by the statements of Venanzi and Varallo Pandolfini, by the absolute denials of Bondesio, and by the testimony of several witnesses who, for reasons of business or service, having seen and known Varallo Pandolfini, Mayner, Spadafora, and Mr. Calvocoressi, would also have seen and known Bondesio if he had been with them at the place of manufacture.

As for the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Genoa, not the slightest useful indication was ever obtained. In any case, these reports from the Ottoman authorities could only be regarded as denunciations having the same force as reports from state authorities, which must be corroborated by formal and regular judicial evidence.

For this reason, the investigating judge was compelled to issue a formal letter rogatory to the Ottoman judicial authorities, in order to make use of the indications contained in those reports, proceeding on the basis that it was necessary to establish Bondesio’s participation in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes within these Royal States, in accordance with Article 347 of the Penal Code; his participation in the manufacture at Constantinople could only be considered as an indication of complicity in the subsequent manufacture at Turin.

This consideration led to the case against Bondesio always being kept separate from that already instituted against Varallo Pandolfini and Venanzi, since it concerned different times, facts, and places, and it would only have been appropriate, under the law, to join them when, and only when it had been established that Bondesio had, in some manner, taken part in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Turin.

The Indictment Chamber of this Court of Appeal, to which the investigating judge’s report was submitted in accordance with Article 79 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, issued a formal letter rogatory to the Ottoman authorities for the execution of the aforementioned inquiries; by order of September 3 last, which the Superior authority transmitted on September 5 to the Ministry of Justice. Some time later, on October 27, 1858, the Superior received notice from that Ministry that, given the organization of the Ottoman courts, one could not expect that the letter rogatory would be executed in a manner comparable to that of the other states of Europe.

Subsequently, on November 23, 1858, part of the requested inquiries was received, namely the examinations of Jean-Baptiste Bongiacci and Aristide Pallattino. While they confirmed the information already obtained regarding Bondesio’s participation in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Constantinople, they declared that they were entirely unaware of his involvement in the manufacture of the said paper money in Turin.

As soon as these responses were received, it became necessary, in the interest of Bondesio, a Sardinian subject who had been held in pre-trial detention for more than six months, to consider whether the proceedings should not go forward on the basis of the evidence that had been obtained, in addition to that resulting from the investigation conducted here, all of which failed to present the slightest indication that Bondesio had participated in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Turin, although they provided important indications of his complicity in the aforementioned manufacture in Constantinople.

The undersigned does not hesitate to acknowledge that there was not only expediency but necessity in proceeding in such a manner, both because the documents still awaited could not have had such an influence as to change the opinion regarding the crime of manufacturing counterfeit Turkish paper money in Turin on the part of Bondesio, since not only the authors of this fabrication and the witnesses heard on the matter excluded him, but this participation was also excluded by Bongiacci and Pallattino, examined in Constantinople, and because, in any case, whatever precautionary measure might have been taken, it would not have prevented the reopening of proceedings under Articles 238 and 36(?) of the Code of Criminal Procedure if the information still expected from Constantinople had provided sufficient grounds; and finally, because the letter rogatory issued by the Indictment Section to the Ottoman authorities, although intended to obtain a thorough procedure through the examination of all persons believed to be informed, could not oblige that Section to the point of suspending for an indefinite period the progress of the investigation against an individual who had been detained for a long time and against whom sufficient indications of guilt had not been gathered, whereas indications excluding such guilt had been obtained.

Accordingly, the Office of the Advocate General, in submitting to the Indictment Section the interrogations of. Bongiacci and Pallattino, requested, in the interest of justice and although the letter rogatory of September 3 had not yet been fully executed that it be pleased to order the return to the investigating judge of these interrogations and the case documents, so that, in accordance with the law, the investigation of the case concerning Bondesio might be brought to a close.

The Indictment Chamber, adhering to the opinion of the Public Prosecutor, by order of November 27 last, granted the aforementioned request. The result of this measure was the order of the Council Chamber of the Tribunal dated November 29, 1858, in accordance with the fiscal conclusions of the same day, by which, considering the lack of proof that Bondesio had continued in Turin to be part of the association in which he had participated in Constantinople concerning the manufacture of counterfeit caimes, it was declared that there was no ground for further proceedings against Bondesio, and his release was ordered.

Bondesio was set at liberty. One month later, that is, on December 29, 1858, the Minister of Justice transmitted to the undersigned the supplementary information relating to the letter rogatory of the Court of Appeal dated September 3. This information consisted of the examinations of Dimitri Calvocoressi and Ferdinand Sebreggi. The former stated that he did not know Advocate Bondesio and had only heard a A. Bresciani say that Bondesio had been arrested in Turin on suspicion of having manufactured counterfeit paper money; the latter declared that he had indeed known Advocate Bondesio in Pera and had even carried out some work in his trade as a goldsmith for the Bondesio family, but at the same time stated that he was unaware that Bondesio had taken part in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes.

Clearly, the aforementioned information does not alter the state of the proceedings; on the contrary, it irrefutably confirms what had already emerged from the earlier evidence, namely the lack not only of proof but even of indications that Bondesio had participated in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Turin. These materials were therefore added to the case file so that they might receive due consideration should further evidence arise against Bondesio, for the purposes contemplated by Article 238 of the aforementioned Code of Criminal Procedure.

The Advocate General was for a moment in doubt as to whether the order of the Council Chamber of November 29 should be notified to the Representative of the Sublime Porte, who had constituted himself as a civil party in the other case against Varallo Pandolfini, Venanzi, and their associates.

However, he soon realized that this case could never be joined with the one being conducted against Bondesio, because the former concerned the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Turin, whereas in the proceedings against Bondesio the matter concerned only acts committed in Constantinople; that the Representative of the Sublime Porte was aware that, in addition to the proceedings against Venanzi and others, a separate case had to be instituted against Bondesio, and even later, since the first case was already nearing completion; and that, notwithstanding this, he had not seen fit to constitute himself as a civil party, although he had the right to do so. No one can be presumed to be a civil party in proceedings, as the law requires a formal and explicit act and a deposit for current expenses under Article 494 of the Code already cited. Moreover, if the Ottoman Representative had been regarded as a civil party in the Bondesio case, the same order of dismissal would have condemned him to reimburse the expenses advanced by the Royal Treasury in accordance with Article 193.

For all these reasons, the Fiscal Advocate, having concluded that the said Representative could not be considered a civil party in the proceedings against Bondesio, did not notify him of the above-mentioned order of dismissal, in accordance with Article 214 of the Code of Criminal Procedure.

Nevertheless, the said Representative of Turkey believes that his constitution as a civil party in the proceedings against Venanzi and Varallo Pandolfini, with the sole deposit of 30 francs, could also serve for the Bondesio case, which began when the other was nearing its end; and he still retains the possibility of filing an opposition under Article 233 of the said Code, since, due to the lack of formal notification, the 24-hour time limit established by Article 214 has not begun to run.

However, given the state of the proceedings, even if the Indictment Section had recognized the said representative as a civil party, the opposition would inevitably have been rejected and followed by his condemnation to costs and expenses under Article 237 of the said Code. This is for the simple reason that absolutely nothing in the proceedings could establish that Bondesio had manufactured counterfeit caimes within the Royal States. The only act attributable to him is participation in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes in Constantinople; but our Penal Code does not punish such conduct. For the manufacture of foreign credit paper to constitute a punishable offense, the falsification must have taken place within the Royal States. The Penal Code does not otherwise provide for the manufacture of counterfeit foreign currency. For there to be a crime, Article 335 of the Penal Code requires that the fabrication occur within the Royal States.

The provisions of the law, however harsh they may appear to the Representative of the Sublime Porte, cannot be violated by the magistrates and courts of the Kingdom, whose duty is to take cognizance only of crimes specified by law and to impose penalties only in the cases and within the limits established by the law (Article 4 of the Penal Code).

Such is the summary which the undersigned is able to submit to the Minister of Grace and Justice, in accordance with the instructions contained in his note of the 11th of the current month, while having the honor to offer him the assurances of his highest respect.

The Fiscal Advocate General
(signed) Persolia
