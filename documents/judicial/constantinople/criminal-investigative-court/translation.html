Minutes of the Criminal Investigative Court at the Imperial Ministry of Police

The authorities having been informed that a woman named Margarita de Petris, wife of the Sardinian subject Francisco de Petris, was circulating counterfeit kaimes (paper money) in her shop located in Pera, a search was conducted at her residence and shop. There were found five hundred counterfeit kaimes of ten piastres.

Consequently, the said Margarita was placed under arrest. When questioned as to from whom she had obtained these kaimes, the accused declared that the said banknotes had been sold to her, 70 at four piastres each, by the Sardinian subject Gaetano Manzo and the Austrian subject Ludovico Boschi.

Following these statements, the two individuals were arrested and brought to the Police Prefecture on the date of 30 Djemadi-el-Akher 1275 (3 February 1859).

The said Manzo, questioned in the presence of the dragoman of the chancery, declared that the counterfeit kaimes were put into circulation by the said Ludovico Boschi; that he himself obtained them from Boschi and spent them; that he had introduced Margarita to Boschi; and that although he did not know where these counterfeit kaimes were manufactured or where they had come from, he believed that this information might be discovered in a letter that had arrived the same day by post addressed to Boschi.

The said letter, having been retrieved from the post office, showed that the kaimes were manufactured by individuals named Raphaele Randa Boschi and Sebastiano Zanetti, residing in Bologna, who sent them to Ludovico Boschi, as well as to the Austrian subjects Roberto Diamanti and Gezri Venissi, and the English subject Andonaki Draganico, all four residing in Constantinople, for circulation.

The said Boschi, likewise questioned in the presence of Mr. Hoffer, dragoman of the chancery, confirmed the contents of the aforementioned letter and declared that the said Raphaele Randa Boschi and Sebastiano had sent him counterfeit kaimes as samples, proposing that, if these papers could be successfully passed into circulation, they would manufacture and send more at a later time.

At the same time that the Imperial Ministry of Foreign Affairs telegraphed the chargé d’affaires of the Porte in Turin and Naples for the arrest of the aforementioned culprits in Bologna, an official was specially dispatched to the location in order to continue the interrogation and trial of the accused and thereby bring the matter to light.

At that time, the said Raphaele Randa Boschi, when questioned, declared that about a year and a half earlier, upon returning from Crimea to Constantinople, he had reached an agreement with Ludovico to procure two genuine banknotes, one old ten-piastre note and another of twenty piastres, and had gone to Bologna where, having partnered with individuals named Tomasso Facchini and Raphaele Crudeli, he had had a lithographer-engraver named Corti engrave a counterfeit twenty-piastre note complete with number and seal on the back. They had likewise printed five to six thousand counterfeit kaimes of twenty piastres in the house of the said Crudeli and had sent two samples to Ludovico, but had not received a reply. Later Facchini, after secretly taking the said kaimes to his home, brought them to Constantinople hidden in a box among toothpicks; that upon arriving in Constantinople he met with Ludovico Boschi and handed the kaimes over to the English subject Andonaki Draganicos to be put into circulation. However, this could not be carried out due to the poor quality of the paper, and they were forced to burn them.

Later, the said Raphaele Boschi, having formed an association in Constantinople with the aforementioned Andonaki, his associate the Austrian subject Gezri Venissi, Roberto Diamanti, and Ludovico Boschi, sought to correct the model of the kaimes. Upon returning to Bologna for this purpose, a dispute arose between Facchini and Crudeli, and mutual trust disappeared; they erased the model and completely burned the kaimes that had been printed at Crudeli’s instigation, except for the numbering and seal, which remained in the hands of the lithographer Corti.

Following this, Crudeli ceased contact with the others and withdrew from the association. After the imprisonment of the lithographer Corti for various offenses, another lithographer, Sebastiano Zanini, was admitted into the group through the mediation of Facchini, Raphaele Boschi, Alexandro Donini, and Vincenzo Busi. After having him draw a model based on an old twenty-piastre kaimé, they printed eight thousand pieces in Facchini’s house, using the numbering and seal that were in Corti’s possession.

They had already sent two thousand of these to their associates in Constantinople, concealed in a box placed among toothpicks. As for the remainder, the said Facchini, as well as Raphaele Randa Boschi and Alexandro Donini, each transported two thousand pieces to Constantinople in their luggage, where they spent them through their associates.

While Facchini was in Constantinople, a thief stole his wallet; at the time, he was attempting to pass thirty-two counterfeit kaimes contained in it. During his interrogation, he declared that he was not a counterfeiter, but that he had found the said items dropped from someone’s pocket and undertook to find the owner. Indeed, the said thief later identified Facchini and had him arrested; however, Facchini was not found guilty and was released.

After this, Facchini went to France and left the group. Raphaele Boschi, returning once more to Bologna, had the model corrected by the lithographer Tom through the intermediary of Zanini, and had the reverse of the same model redesigned based on a ten-piastre note. In this way, they printed several thousand counterfeit kaimes of twenty and ten piastres. On two occasions, they sent twelve thousand ten-piastre notes to their associates in Constantinople in crates containing lard, and later six thousand twenty-piastre notes by the same method.

Raphaele Boschi, having stopped at Smyrna on his last journey to Bologna, met there with Andonaki, from whom he received nine watches as payment for previously dispatched kaimes, as well as two genuine new twenty-piastre notes intended to serve as models for a new issue. He delivered these to Zanini upon arriving in Bologna, and then to Tom for engraving. After purchasing the necessary engraving equipment, they engraved and drew the models of the said kaimes, after which they sent two printed samples to Constantinople. As for the price of the counterfeit ten and twenty-piastre kaimes most recently dispatched, their associates in Constantinople, namely Ludovico Boschi, Roberto Diamanti, Gezri Venissi, and Andonaki Draganicos, as well as Alexandro Donini and Vincenzo, were to share it with those residing in Bologna, namely Raphaele Boschi, Sebastiano Zanini, and Vincenzo Busi.

Finally, a certain sum was also to be paid as compensation to the engraver Tom, as well as to Zanini’s brother-in-law, Antonio Dareni, who had assisted in printing the counterfeit kaimes.

The authorities, having learned that Alessandro Donini was in Constantinople, except for Vincenzo Busi, who had fled to Sardinia and could not be found despite all searches, had Raphaele Crudeli, Enrico Forti, Tomasso Facchini, Sebastiano Zanini, Raphaele Randa Boschi, and Tom arrested. When questioned, each of them confirmed, insofar as he knew, the statements and confessions of the aforementioned Raphaele Randa Boschi.

Likewise, the authorities, having learned that a man named Ulysses Baldini, who had been part of the counterfeiting band of Baptiste Bongiaci and who had not previously been located, was in Bologna, had him arrested and placed in custody.

When interrogated, Baldini at first denied the charges against him, but eventually confessed that he was associated with the counterfeiters known in Constantinople and that he had engraved the seal and a cipher (tughra).

An Ottoman official who had been sent on this mission to Bologna, after receiving from the Advocate General, president of the criminal court there, a formal undertaking that the models of the old ten and twenty-piastre kaimes, as well as those of the cipher and the seal, would be sent to Constantinople following the conviction of the accused, returned here carrying three models of new kaimes, the machine used to engrave them, two of the watches that Randa Boschi had taken from Andonaki, and finally the minutes of the interrogations and other documents of accusation duly legalized by the local authorities.

Meanwhile, the accused Ludovico Boschi, who was imprisoned in the police prefecture of Constantinople, retracted his earlier confessions and issued the most absolute denials. Likewise, the other accused persons also completely denied the charges against them. The interrogation records drawn up in Bologna were read to them one by one in the presence of their respective dragomans. Ludovico Boschi refused entirely to listen to the reading, adding:

“As I am in no way guilty of the acts imputed to me, I consider all the testimony and all the evidence brought against me to be false; I will not accept them and will say absolutely nothing.”

The said Gezri Venissi, for his part, spoke the following words:

“I became involved in this affair by chance and derived no profit from it. If I had anything to say on my own account, I would have said it. I repeat, it was by chance that I became mixed up in the affair and I gained no benefit; and although I know who took part in it and profited from it, I will nevertheless say nothing. Therefore do not insist in vain, for I will not speak. I submit to my fate and to whatever you decide regarding me.”

For their part, Roberto Diamanti and Andonaki Draganicos, after hearing everything imputed to them in the interrogations conducted in Bologna, said: “It is true that our names are mentioned in these documents, but it is not us; perhaps they are other individuals bearing the same names as ours. If you confront us with them, we are certain that we would be completely unknown to them.”

Since such a confrontation was impossible, it was decided to send them to Pera to have their portraits taken by photography in the presence of their respective dragomans. The said Gezri Venissi, Roberto Diamanti, and Andonaki Draganicos allowed their portraits to be taken, with the exception of Ludovico Boschi, who said to the photographer:

“As long as my portrait remains on that plate, I will keep yours in my heart.”

He likewise said to another draftsman who had been summoned:

“I do not want my portrait to be made. If it is for money that you wish to do it, I will give you some; but if you persist, I will soon leave prison, and then think about it!”

In view of these threats and the impossibility of obtaining the accused’s portrait, the matter was dropped. After marking number one on the portrait of Andonaki Draganicos, number two on that of Gezri Venissi, and number three on that of Roberto Diamanti, the photographs were sent to Bologna through the Imperial Ministry of Foreign Affairs to be shown to the accused there, in order to determine who could be identified, what their names were, and what relations existed among them.

According to the reply that has just been received, Sebastiano Zanini, after examining the said portraits, made the following declaration:

“I have not been to Constantinople, nor have they come here. I know nothing of them. I learned the names of our associates in Constantinople only from Ludovico Boschi, first, who had already come here, and secondly from Raphaele Randa Boschi and Tomasso Facchini, and also because I saw them written in correspondence; but personally, I do not know them.”

Tomasso Facchini said:

“As I have already stated in my interrogation, I saw these individuals only once in Constantinople. Therefore, I cannot distinguish them one by one or assign each of them a name.”

As for Raphaele Randa Boschi, he declared that he recognized number one as the portrait of Andonaki Draganicos, number three as that of Gezri Venissi, the associate of the said Andonaki, and number two as that of Roberto Diamanti, adding:

“As I have already stated in my interrogation, these are the individuals who are our associates.”

The Internunciature reported, in writing, the death of Roberto Diamanti in the embassy hospital. As for the Austrian subject Ludovico Boschi, he remains in prison. Regarding the other Austrian subjects, Gezri Venissi (also of the same government) and the English subject Andonaki Draganicos, they were handed over to the authorities of their respective chancelleries in accordance with the applicable written orders and regulations. After the necessary guarantees had been provided in writing (tahrir) by their embassies, they were transferred to their respective chancelleries. However, although they have since been officially requested again from the said chancelleries in accordance with the terms of those guarantees, they have not, up to this day, been returned to the authorities.

The prison records of the police have shown that these individuals have no prior criminal history.

In summary: The Austrian subject Ludovico Boschi, imprisoned under the charge of counterfeiting, when interrogated in the presence of his dragoman, initially made confessions. Although he later withdrew into denials, since it was on the basis of his earlier confessions that an official had been sent to Bologna and since he was the cause of the discovery of the counterfeiting ring there, his denials cannot be accepted and are considered entirely without foundation. He is therefore found guilty of the crime of counterfeiting.

Likewise, although the denials of the Austrian subject Roberto Diamanti and the English subject Andonaki Draganicos assert that they took no part in the circulation of counterfeit money, nevertheless, in view of the seized letters, the statements of the accused imprisoned in Bologna, and the fact that during the presentation in Bologna of the photographic portraits of the accused in Constantinople, Raphaele Randa Boschi declared and confirmed the resemblance of the portraits to the aforementioned individuals, their denials are likewise considered entirely unfounded.

With regard to Gezri Venissi, since his statement that he became involved in the affair by chance and without profit, and his refusal to reveal those who actually took part and benefited from the crime, demonstrate that he too was involved in the matter, he is accordingly found to have participated in the crime.

As for the accused in Bologna: considering the confession of Tomasso Facchini; considering that although Raphaele Crudeli had not remained part of the group and had left it four years earlier, he nevertheless admitted that he had previously been associated with some of the said accused in counterfeiting; considering the confession of Enrico Forti that he had taken part in the association and personally engraved the models; considering that Raphaele Randa Boschi made full confessions and is recognized as the leader of the group; considering the statements of Sebastiano Zanini that he himself printed the counterfeit kaimes in his capacity as a lithographer and that he had been associated with and part of the group; considering that Antonio Dareni, brother-in-law of the said Sebastiano and a lithographer, assisted in printing the counterfeit kaimes, although he was not formally part of the group, yet having been, according to his own confession, won over by the accused and having accepted a watch and a sum of money as profit, he is also guilty in this respect; considering that Guglielmo Tom, although he engraved the models for pay and at the request of the said Sebastiano, declared that he was unaware that this was for the manufacture of counterfeit kaimes and did not know the purpose of the engraved characters on the models; considering that Ulysses Baldini did not form part of the group, but had been involved in the manufacture of counterfeit kaimes with counterfeiters arrested three years earlier in Trieste and sentenced each to three years of hard labor (galley service), by engraving the numbering and the seal on the reverse; considering that the fugitives (contumaces) Vincenzo Busi, Vincenzo Arribuggi, and Alexandre Donini are all three recognized as guilty counterfeiters on the basis of the depositions of the accused in Bologna; considering that everything mentioned above regarding the accused in Bologna is supported by the interrogation records transmitted from that city, as well as by the oral statements of the official sent on this mission;

And as regards the Englishman Andonaki Draganicos and the Austrian Gezri Venissi, although, as stated above, they were handed over to their respective chancelleries at the request of their embassies and by superior order, under the obligation and commitment undertaken by those embassies to deliver them to the authorities upon first request, nevertheless, up to this day Andonaki has not been handed over; and as for Gezri Venissi, his embassy has replied that the guarantor of the accused having become a Sardinian subject, it was necessary to address the chancery of that government. This having been done, the Sardinian chancery in turn officially replied that the said guarantor had some time ago left Constantinople for other countries; considering the death of the Austrian Roberto Diamanti in prison, there remains under the protection of the said government only the aforementioned Ludovico Boschi;

For all these reasons, the Court finds and declares Raphaele Randa Boschi, Sebastiano Zanini, Tomasso Facchini, and Enrico Forti to be counterfeiters, and Antonio Dareni to be an accomplice in counterfeiting. As for Raphaele Crudeli and the engraver Tom: the former had initially been part of the group by allowing the counterfeit kaimes to be manufactured in his house; however, having repented before their discovery, he burned them completely and destroyed and erased the models. The latter, it has been proven, engraved the models in complete ignorance. Consequently, the Court defers to the superior authority regarding the measures to be taken with respect to these two accused.

The Court likewise finds the accused connected with Bologna, namely the Austrians Ludovico Boschi and Gezri Venissi, and the Englishman Andonaki Draganicos, guilty of having put counterfeit kaimes into circulation, and Ulysses Baldini as an associate and engraver for counterfeiters previously sentenced to three years of hard labor.

Accordingly, all these accused counterfeiters imprisoned in Constantinople and Bologna, numbering eight, together with the three fugitives, are sentenced to the penalty prescribed in Article 148 of the Penal Code; and the three accomplices in the circulation of counterfeit kaimés to the penalty prescribed in Article 143 of the same code.

The Supreme Court of Justice shall, in accordance with its authority, determine the duration of the sentences of the aforementioned convicted persons.

Done at Constantinople on 16 Djemadi el-Akher 1277. Follow the seals of the president and the members of the Court.

Record of Interrogation of Two Individuals Accused of Manufacturing Paper Money

Interrogation of the individual named Ludovico Boschi, an Austrian subject, conducted after he was brought to the Ministry of Police on February 9, 1859 (afternoon).

Q: You have been brought here in connection with counterfeit caimés (paper money); tell us everything you know about this matter.
A: My uncle, Raphael Boschi of Bologna, came during the past month of September from Smyrna to Constantinople with the aim of forming a company for the manufacture and distribution of counterfeit caimes. We met with him and agreed that he would send me counterfeit ten-piastre notes from Bologna, which I would circulate here and send him the proceeds. He stayed here about twenty days and then returned to Bologna.

From there, he wrote me several letters, to which I replied on the 24th of last December. I sent him 1,150 piastres on the 30th of the same month, and 2,875 on January 24, 1849, making a total of 5,274 piastres.

He sent me six to seven thousand counterfeit ten-piastre notes. I received these counterfeit notes on December 21 and sold them here and there. Finally, by the last Austrian steamship, he sent me, enclosed in a letter, two new counterfeit twenty-piastre notes, writing to me: “Please see if they are good, so that I may send you more.”

I found them good and replied asking him to send me more, but I have not yet received a response.

(Signed) Ludovico Boschi
(Signed) Interpreter of the Interrogation (Austrian)

Later, during the Imperial months (unclear wording), seeing that many people frequented Feriköy, we had the idea of opening a drink shop. We did so and earned something, but when customers became scarce, we were left without work, and the money we had earned ran out.

I then worked as a servant and washerwoman in a house in that area to make a living, and my husband worked for a Sardinian butcher named Parmesani, bringing him pigs from Varna for a salary of one thousand piastres per month. He went there about a month ago and returned last Saturday.

But as our debts to the baker, the shopkeeper, and for rent had already reached 700 piastres, and I had no money, my husband went to the aforementioned Parmesani to collect his wages.

One of the people who used to frequent our shop, whose name and profession I did not know, came and said to me: “Will you give me the key to that cupboard which remains locked, so that I may put some items there?” I gave it to him. I did not see what he put inside, as I was busy with my work. He took the key and left.

A young man who for several days had been pestering me, saying, “You have papers,” and although I told him I was not such a woman and had nothing of the kind, came back several times. Finally, last night he came accompanied by some gendarmes. They searched my house and found nothing; but at last they asked me for the key to the said cupboard, and when I told them I did not have it, they broke the lock, took some things from inside, and brought me to the police with them.

Q: When that man brought and placed those objects in the cupboard, did you not tell your husband? And when did you tell him?
A: No, I did not have time to tell him; he is unaware of it.

Q: How long did those objects remain in your house?
A: I do not know whether he put anything there or not. I gave him the key in the morning, and in the evening my house was searched by the police; that is why I did not have time to speak about it to my husband.

Q: You cannot clear yourself with such statements. This is a matter on which the government has already obtained reliable information; therefore, you have no other course but to tell the truth. Tell the truth.
A: Seven or eight months earlier, I was occupied selling drinks in the shop located beneath the house of Vaccaro. An Italian, a Sardinian subject, named Gaetano Manzo, who frequented our shop with others to drink, told me one day that he had “papers” and that he wanted to give me some so that I too could make a profit from them. Taking from his pocket five to ten counterfeit ten-piastre caimes, he gave them to me at 4 piastres each, which I in turn gave to a certain Master Ottone at 4 1⁄2 piastres.

I took from Gaetano three or four more times, each time ten to fifteen pieces, which I always gave to the same person. My husband knew nothing about it. I also learned from Gaetano himself that he obtained these papers from a man named Ludovico Boschi, an Austrian subject.

A dispute had arisen over accounts between Boschi and Gaetano, and as the latter did not bring me any papers for one or two months, I myself strongly urged Boschi, who used to come to our place, to give me some. He handed me, taking them from his pocket, about fifty notes of ten piastres each, always at the price of four piastres, which I in turn sold at four and a half piastres to Master Ottone.

I continued, each day, to take from Boschi and to give to Master Ottone fifty of these counterfeit caimes. It was yesterday that Ottone came to ask me for 500 pieces, and I took from Boschi the pieces that were brought here, which I was keeping to deliver to Ottone after an hour.

It was following the instructions of Boschi and Gaetano that I placed those pieces where the police came to find them yesterday, not keeping them in my house for fear that a search might be made.

Q: You say that your husband knew nothing about it! That is impossible.
A: At the beginning, when I was taking these counterfeit caimes from Gaetano, I told him about it, and he became angry and wanted to beat me. After that, I did not take any for two or three months. It is only now, when my husband was absent, that I began taking them from Boschi, and I told my husband nothing about it when he returned.

Q: The police officer took some of these papers from your husband as well. What do you say to that?
A: It is impossible that he took any. My husband gave me all the money he had; I never saw money on him, so he could not have given any to anyone.

Q: But a new piece of these papers was found on your husband.
A: It is impossible. If my husband had such a piece, even if I had not seen it, he would have shown it to me himself. Therefore, I cannot believe that such a piece was found on him.

Q: The man named Clitzi-Cole who lives in your house, how much does he know about these matters?
A: He knows nothing about them. He has no knowledge at all.

Q: How long has that man been staying with you, and for what reason did he come?

A: He is my compatriot. He stays with me, paying 18 to 20 piastres per day for his food and other expenses.

Interrogation of Gaetano Manzoni in the presence of the dragomans Mr. Hoffler and Mr. Baron
18 Redjeb 1275

Q. What is your name and your father’s name? What is your country, and to which power are you subject?
A. My name is Gaetano Manzoni; my father’s name is Pietro; I am from Genoa, a Sardinian subject.

Q. What do you know about the matter of the counterfeit caimes? Who are the people involved in this affair? Tell us everything from beginning to end.
A. Last year, in the spring, I was at Cesare’s, working for Parmesani as a coachman. With Parmesani there was a clerk named Ludovico Boschi. About a month later, one day Boschi said to me: “I have as many as twenty thousand counterfeit caimes; come and I will give you some so that you can pass them.” I asked him to give me three or four and to tell me the price. He gave me some pieces, and we agreed on a price of 4 piastres. I sold up to 700 pieces to Margarita de Petris, to Ottone, to Mᵗᵒ, and to Carlato, at 5 to 6 piastres each. One day, during the month of July last year, the said Carlato met me and told me he had some watches, asking whether I wanted to take them in exchange for counterfeit caimes. I replied that I would have to consult the proper person. Then we went up together toward Taksim, and after leaving Carlato on the road to Feriköy near the Protestant cemetery, I went to Boschi and asked him whether he wanted to buy the seven watches Carlato had given me, three of them silver and four plated. Boschi replied that he would buy them for 2,200 piastres, after consultation with Carlato.

I told Boschi to count the money, and he gave me 400 counterfeit caimes of 20 piastres each, which I handed over to Carlato at 5 piastres and 10 paras apiece. As Boschi had told me he would give me the rest the next day, I in turn told this to Carlato. But the next day, when I went to Boschi to collect the remainder, he said the watches were not worth that price and that he would not pay the rest. After I protested, he gave me fifty more counterfeit caimés, declaring that he would give nothing further.

I managed to persuade Carlato and satisfy him by giving him 76 pieces, and thus the remainder stayed with me. When Carlato gave me those watches, he told me they were stolen, that they had been reported to all watchmakers, and therefore should not be shown openly. I reported this to Boschi, who replied that he should not sell them here, but would send them to Bologna.

Later, when I told Boschi that the caimes he had given me were not worth the agreed sum of 2,200 piastres, a dispute arose between us and I left him. Carlato, having learned that Boschi had taken the watches and given the caimes, got in touch with him directly and obtained counterfeit caimes from him; I do not know how many.

Three or four months ago, I met Boschi at Taksim near Pangaltı and asked him how things were going. He replied that he was expecting from outside that very day some counterfeit 10-piastre caimes. I asked him the price; he said they were expensive, at 8 piastres. I told him I would not take them even at 3 piastres. A few days later, when the theft affair came to light, I went to de Petris’ shop to make the iron pieces, which I produced with Giovanni Killo. We went to Galata, but we were unable to open the shop we had planned.

Much later, I went again to de Petris’ shop. Boschi had received from the interior counterfeit 10-piastre caimes. De Petris, his wife, and I realized that I was of no use, that is, we had failed to open the Galata shop because the shop foreman had suspected something. So he dismissed me as useless, and I then took employment as a servant in the Prado café in Pera, at Beyoğlu, near Taksim, opposite the barracks.

Q. How do you know that Boschi had received counterfeit caimes and that they had passed into the hands of Margarita? Tell us everything you know about this matter.
A. About an hour after de Petris, following the dispute he had raised with me, dismissed me, he and his wife, he called me back an hour later and told me that Boschi had received from outside counterfeit 10-piastre caimes, and that if I wished, we could pass them together. He added that the reason he had sent for me was the presence of Cilitzi, because he did not want him to be involved with us, as that would reduce the profit.

I also knew that the counterfeit caimes had arrived because, while I was making the iron pieces, I had asked Boschi for money, and he gave me 60 piastres in counterfeit 10-piastre caimes. I also heard that the same people to whom I had previously given counterfeit 20-piastre caimes were already receiving 10-piastre pieces from de Petris. When I asked Cilitzi, in de Petris’ shop itself, he told me he knew nothing about it, but that since Margarita was constantly spending 10-piastre caimes, they must indeed have arrived.

As for me, I neither took nor passed counterfeit 10-piastre caimes except for the 60 piastres I had received from Boschi, which I spent through Clitzi, who, however, did not know that they were counterfeit.

Q. Was Cilitzi aware of the counterfeit caime affair?
A. I presume he was, because he told me that Margarita was spending counterfeit 10-piastre caimes. But I cannot say whether he himself took or passed any of these counterfeit caimes, whether of twenty or ten piastres.

Q. Who are Boschi’s associates in the counterfeit caime affair, and what are the names of those among them who are in Constantinople? Tell us.
A. One day I asked Cilitzi how Boschi managed to bring these counterfeit caimes here. He replied that they were hidden in some secret place, inside barrels filled with salted provisions or sardines. These barrels were sent overland to Trieste. Boschi then shipped them by steamer to Constantinople.

I asked how they were taken out of customs. He told me that there was an employee at the customs office who had been bribed or arranged with, and thus they were passed through easily. As for Boschi’s associates in Constantinople, I know nothing.

Q. Have you not even suspected anyone of being associated with Boschi?

A. A man named Cesar Venissi, an Austrian subject, and Antonaki, an English subject, used to go very often to Boschi’s house and were in contact with him. One day I asked Boschi for some counterfeit caimes. He took me with him, and we went together to Beyoğlu, where he made me sit in a café called Bublul, telling me to wait there while he went to the “Petit-Champ” to get the caimes.

I knew that Venissi’s house was at the Petit-Champ, so I waited for a while, and when he returned, he gave me two counterfeit 20-piastre caimes, for which I paid him. Another day I asked Boschi for 200 counterfeit caimes. He told me to go to Pera, where he would give them to me, and he led me through four streets, then told me to wait for him there. I sat down at the Teke café. Shortly afterward, I saw Boschi coming from the direction of Venissi’s house, accompanied by Venissi and Antonaki, and I followed them as far as Taksim.

We went to a spirits factory located opposite the Taksim fountain, where each of us had a drink. After leaving and setting off again, on Monday, Boschi took a bundle of counterfeit caimes from his pocket and handed them to me, saying that it was one hundred pieces. When I pointed out that I had asked him for 200, he replied that his associates would not agree to him giving me more than 100.

Q. Do you know a man named Robert Diamanti, an Austrian? Was he one of the associates?
A. I do not know him, and I am unaware whether he is one of the associates.

Q. Do you know a man named Nito, a Latin subject?
A. Yes, I know him, and I gave him counterfeit caimes.

Q. Does de Petris have full knowledge of this affair?
A. Yes, he does. When he was working as a farrier at Taksim opposite a French shop, he sometimes went to Feriköy, where from time to time he would ask his wife and me for counterfeit caimes, which I gave him. Thus, he had full knowledge of it and spent them; at other times we went together to cafés and shops, where I spent them in his presence and he saw it.

Then I, Manzo, Ludovico, Giovanni, Romani, Maltese Clitzi Cole, Vetro, Antonio, and Jeani Helleni all met, consulted together, and in due time made the smaller iron tools.

However, we realized we needed larger tools to open one of the chests more easily. I told them I would take care of making them myself if they provided the necessary funds. Ludovico gave me ten piastres, and Clitzi Cole sixty piastres. Q. If Clitzi the Maltese were to deny this fact, how could you prove it?
A. This concerns a matter that one would not ordinarily dare to speak about with someone with whom one has no relationship. Besides, even if Clitzi the Maltese claims that he does not know me, the Maltese woman who owns the room we wanted to rent will confirm that we went to her place with him. If she is questioned, her statement will show that we were in contact with him and that we were together every day.

Interrogation of Soufiano Makri, British subject, otherwise called “Maltese Clitzi”

Q. What is your name? What country are you from, and what is your nationality?
A. My name is Soufiano Makri; my father’s name is Giovanni. I am Maltese and a British subject.

Q. How old are you?
A. I am thirty-six years old.

Q. How long have you been in Constantinople?
A. I have been here for eighteen years, though I often went to Malta and returned.

Q. Are you married or single?
A. I am unmarried.

Q. What is your profession?
A. I lend money at interest and also deal in the sale of goods that I import from Malta.

Q. Where do you spend your days, and which people do you associate with?
A. I had previously opened a shop in the vicinity of a tavern in Galata, and I had as a partner a certain Giovanni Estepante. I remained there afterward and occupied myself with lending money at interest to prostitutes, staying near the brothels in Galata.

Q. You used to go to the shop of a Maltese man located near the Sardinian Hospital, what is his name? A. Before I was imprisoned, I used to go to Gaetano’s shop and lend him money at interest.

Q. Where did you make the acquaintance of Clitzi Cole, a Sardinian subject?
A. I do not know him; I might recognize him if I saw him.

Q. Do you know Cole Jani Giovanni Romani?
A. I might recognize him if I saw him.

Q. Do you not know Gezri Venissi and Ludovic Boschi?
A. I saw Gezri Venissi once in the Café du Roco, located on the main street of Galata, and I met him in the street once or twice. But I do not know the individual named Ludovico Boschi; I heard someone call Gezri Venissi by that name in the café, and that is how I learned his name.

Q. It follows, then, from what you have just said that you have no relations with him.
A. I have no relations with him; I have not even spoken to him.

Q. You also do not know Petro and Antonio Makri?
A. If I saw them, I might recognize them.

Q. You surely know Gaetano Manzo?
A. Yes, I know him.

Q. Since when have you known him?
A. For four months.

Q. Where did you first make his acquaintance?
A. I saw him for the first time in Galata, in the shop of a certain Benito Manzo, an English subject.

Q. You intended to rent a room with Gaetano Manzo, would you have lived there together?
A. No. There was no discussion between us about renting any room; I had my own lodging separately.

Q. Did you not see the landlady of the room and speak with her in Maltese?
A. No. Such a thing did not happen.

Q. What will you say if this lady comes and tells you to your face that you asked for the room?
A. If she says so, I will never be able to get out of prison.

Q. Is it true that Clitzi Cole, Gaetano, and you agreed, in the shop you mentioned earlier, to open a shop, break open the chests there, and take the money they contained?
A. That was never discussed. However, one day Gaetano came to me asking for a rope ladder, and I drove him away. It is because of that that he bears me a grudge and has said such things.

Q. What would he have done with that ladder?
A. It was to enter the room of a lady who was always alone.

Q. It appears that such rope ladders are found in your possession?
A. There are none at my place; he told me that since I had dealings with sailors and Maltese people, he had asked me to obtain one for him through them.

Q. Clitzi and Gaetano made some iron tools—you saw them, did you not?
A. I only saw them here, in the Pasha’s residence.

Q. You and Clitzi showed a shop to Gaetano, who did it belong to?
A. That is false.

Q. You held a meeting in a shop opposite the Sardinian Hospital, who attended it, and what did you discuss?
A. That is unfounded; no meeting took place.

The aforementioned individuals, having been confronted with one another, maintained their previous statements, and the said Maltese Soufiano Makri repeated that he would make no further statement if any of the facts reported by Gaetano were proven.

Interrogation of Giovanni Romano, Sardinian subject

Q. What is your name? What is your father’s name? What country are you from, and what is your nationality?
A. My name is Giovanni Romano; my father’s name is Victor Romano. I am from the town of Luggo in Sardinia and am a Sardinian subject.

Q. How old are you? A. I am about 25 to 26 years old.

Q. What is your profession?
A. I am a mechanic.

Q. How long have you been in Constantinople?
A. For six years.

Q. Where were you established during that time, and what did you do?
A. I worked for nine months in the workshop of Mr. Maurice in Galata; four years in Perin’s factory at Yeni Çelebi; and during that period I left from time to time to work in other workshops. During the war, I entered the service of the English who were working at Sarayburnu, and afterward I worked in a gauze factory. More recently, I was working for Mr. Perin aboard an Ottoman steamship.

Q. Do you know Gezri, a Sardinian subject?
A. I met him last year at Parmesani’s, when he was engaged in assembling a soda-water (carbonated lemonade) machine.

Q. Have you been to his shop at Feriköy?
A. Yes, I have been there.

Q. Do you know Gaetano Manzo, a Sardinian subject?
A. When I was working on Parmesani’s machine, Gaetano was in his service, that is when I saw him.

Q. You and Gaetano did something in de Petris’ shop, what was it?
A. Nothing, sir, we did nothing.

Q. Have you ever seen Gaetano Manzo in de Petris’ shop?
A. Last year we went with him two or three times to de Petris’ shop, and we drank together there.

Continued interrogation of Giovanni Romani

Q. There is a certain Gezri Venissi, an Austrian subject, do you know him?
A. No, I do not know him.

Q. Do you know Ludovico Boschi, an Austrian subject?
A. I knew him when he was in the service of Parmesani.

Q. Is it true that you received money from Gezri Venissi, that is, one pound?
A. No. How could I receive money from someone I do not know?

Q. Is it true that you made some small iron instrument?
A. I worked last year at Boschi’s on a machine for carbonated lemonade, but I did not make any other instruments.

Interrogation of Vasil Athanasia, employee in the distillery of Serandi Leonide (a Greek subject)

Q. What is your name? What is your father’s name? What is your native country, and what is your nationality?
A. My name is Vasile; my father’s name is Athanasia. I am a Greek subject, and I am twenty years old.

Q. Where do you work?
A. I am employed in the distillery of Leonide Serandi, which is located on the main street of Galata.

Q. Who brought you the large iron pieces that were discovered in your factory and taken to the police?
A. A Frenchman, passing by the factory one day, left them there, saying he would return to pick them up in half an hour. About three months passed without him taking them back. However, about a month after he had left the iron pieces at the factory, he passed by again; I urged him to take them back, but he replied that he could not because he was busy working aboard a steamship. I then assumed they would be abandoned there. He repeated that since they were only a few iron pieces, it would not matter.

Q. What is the name of this Frenchman?
A. I do not know.

Q. How many times did he come to the factory?
A. He came once before leaving the iron pieces, once when he left them with me, and once afterward, so he came three times in total.

Q. How do you know he was French?
A. I took him to be French because he spoke to me in that language, but I do not know his nationality.

Q. Would you recognize him if you saw him?
A. I would recognize him.

Q. Have you not seen him again, at least in the street, after the iron pieces were taken to the police?
A. No, I have not seen him again.

Q. What is his description?
A. He is of standard height. It was he himself who brought the small iron pieces, which are in the suitcase, to the factory, while the long iron pieces were brought by a tall man.

When Clitzi Cole appeared before the tribunal, the said Vasil pointed him out, saying that he was the one who had brought the suitcase to the factory. The long pieces had been brought, as he himself admitted, by the Sardinian subject Gaetano Manzo, who, being ill, was not presented to Vasil.

But when the matter was put to Clitzi, he replied that one day, having met Manzo at Pera carrying in one hand an object wrapped in cloth and in the other another long object similarly wrapped, he had asked him to accompany and help him by taking the smaller package. They then went together to Galata, where he handed it back to Manzo in a shop located on the main street. Clitzi added that he did not know whether these objects were handed over to the shopkeeper or not; in any case, it was not he who gave them to him.

Resumption of the interrogation of Clitzi Cole

Q. Had you not previously been in that shop? And did you perhaps know Vasil, the employee there?
A. I had been going there for six months and I saw that boy there.

Q. Did Vasil know your name and nationality?
A. I do not know whether he knew them or not.

Q. You yourself handed the suitcase you were carrying to Vasil. Moreover, you told him to be careful that it should not get wet.
A. No, I did not hand it to him, nor did I give him such an instruction.

Q. The object you said was a package was in fact a suitcase. Admit the truth.
A. No, it was not a suitcase, but indeed a package.

When Vasil was questioned in the presence of Clitzi Cole, he replied that the suitcase containing the small iron pieces had been carried by Clitzi and handed over by him; but that it was the tall man accompanying him, who had brought the large pieces, who told him to take care that they should not get wet. He further added that when, a month after these items had been left with him, he told them to take them back, both men were present.

Clitzi denied this, saying that Vasil’s statement was false, and that it was again out of resentment against him that Manzo claimed he had accompanied him to the said shop (factory).

Interrogation of Ludovico Boschi

Q. What is your name and your father’s name? What is your country, your nationality, and your age?
A. My name is Ludovico Boschi; my father’s name is Aporio. I am 37 years old. I am from a village near Bologna in the Papal States; I am an Austrian subject.

Q. When did you arrive in Constantinople?
A. It will be six years this coming August since I arrived in Constantinople.

Q. For what purpose did you come to Constantinople?
A. To see the country.

Q. During all that time, what did you do?
A. I worked in carpentry for nine months with a certain Diomanide, a carpenter in Beşiktaş. On August 19, I accompanied the Franco-English armies, first to Gallipoli, then to Varna, Balaklava, Kamış, and Kerch, where I sold drinks. I returned to Constantinople two and a half years later.

Q. Did you have associates, and who were they?
A. For the first nine or ten months, I had as a partner a certain Veronise, a Pole. Our partnership ended, and I then partnered with Luigi Diomanide, my compatriot, who had been my servant. I separated from him after eight months, and later partnered again with the said Diomanide at Kerch. After working with him for six to seven months, I left him as well and practiced my trade alone until my return to Constantinople.

Q. After you returned to Constantinople, what did you do?
A. After purchasing the furnishings of the hotel called “Siciliano,” I ran a hotel in partnership, first with Cazati for four months, and then with a certain Pala for three months, making a total of seven months. Later, I went to Kerch to bring back to Constantinople some belongings I had left there. After staying there for two and a half months, I returned and entered the service of a man named Marquisi, a Greek subject, who kept the hotel called “Nestano” in Galata. After serving him for four to five months as a servant, I planned to go to India, but thinking I might never return, I went back to Bologna, my homeland, where I stayed for three and a half months. I then returned to Constantinople after visiting Lombardy. However, since a certain Terizoli, who was supposed to accompany me to India, had already departed before my return, I remained here.

Resumption of the interrogation of Ludovico Boschi

Q. What did you do after your return here?
A. I worked as a clerk for Parmesani.

Q. Do you know Angelo Gennari?
A. No, I do not know him, nor have I seen him.

Q. Do you know a certain Raphael Boschi?
A. Yes, he is my uncle.

Q. After you left your homeland, where did you see him?
A. I saw him while I was at Kamış, where there was a lot of business. I wrote to him that if he wanted to make money, he should come there. He came, and that is where I saw him again.

Q. Did you have dealings with him there?
A. No. I was occupied with my own affairs, and he ran a café and a restaurant.

Q. Did you return to Constantinople with him?
A. No, I returned alone. He remained at Kamış, and only after making money there did he return and go back to his country.

Q. Where were you after he returned to his country?
A. I was in Kerch.

Q. Where did you see him afterward?
A. After his return to Constantinople from Bologna, when he was working as a laborer in Galata, I saw him from time to time, but I did not greet him. One day he came to see me in Parmesani’s shop after returning from Bologna, where he had gone a second time, and he received 115 piastres from me, saying he would repay me the next day. I learned that same day that he had left for his homeland. Since then, I have not seen him again.

Q. Were you in contact with him afterward, by correspondence or otherwise?
A. No.

Q. Upon your return to your country, with whom were you in contact besides your family?
A. I saw everyone there, but since I had no particular business, I did not associate with anyone.

Q. Do you know the reason for your detention?
A. I have heard it is because of counterfeit papers, but I have never been involved in such matters.

Q. When you first appeared here, you said that counterfeit papers were being sent to you from Bologna and that you knew about them. Why do you deny it now?
A. I had never been imprisoned in my life, and when I was put in prison, fear overcame me. Manzo, who was detained with me, told me that if I protested my innocence, I would be left to rot in the dungeon, but if, on the contrary, I claimed to be guilty, I would be transferred to the main prison, where I could live more freely. I was thus persuaded to conceal the truth, so everything I said at that time was nothing but lies.

Q. Following your first confession, you were removed from the police and transferred to the ordinary prison. When you appeared a second time before the Tribunal, you denied what you had previously said, but then you confirmed your first confession again in the presence of Mr. Dragoman Hoffer. If it was not true, why did you confirm it?
A. No. I have always denied it after my first interrogation.

(It was then remarked:) This is strange; your first confession, whose truth you now dispute, has been fully confirmed by individuals who were arrested in Bologna through the efforts of an official sent there. The truth has been brought to light. How can you deny it?

At this denial, which appeared inadmissible, Ludovico was invited to listen to the reading of the interrogation records and depositions. However, even before hearing them, he declared that since he was not guilty, everything recorded against him was false and that he rejected it.

Interrogation of Gezri Venissi

Q. What is your name and your father’s name? From what country are you, and what is your nationality?
A. My name is Gezri Venissi, and my father’s name is Giacomo. I am from Livorno, I am 43 years old, and I am under Austrian protection.

Q. How long has it been since you left your country?
A. It has been ten years since I left my country and went directly to Alexandria, where I stayed for two years. From there I went to Smyrna, where I remained for three months, and I arrived in Constantinople at the beginning of the Eastern War. I accompanied the English army to Varna and to Crimea as an interpreter. But having received news of my father’s death, who was in Alexandria, I returned there to see my mother, whom I thought I would find there. She had left the city fifteen days earlier, so I had to leave as well, and I returned here after staying there only two weeks. Three to eight months later, I went to Odessa and to the Principalities of Wallachia and Moldavia to seek employment. As winter arrived, I had to remain in Bucharest until the end of the season, and after a year I returned again to Constantinople.

Q. What is your profession, and what have you done since being in Constantinople?
A. I am a commercial clerk. In Constantinople I worked sometimes as a clerk, sometimes as a small merchant. In all the business I have conducted over the last five years, I had as a partner a certain Andonaki, a Greek by origin, with whom I lived in the same room, we even slept in the same bed and ate together. We worked together in a carpentry shop at Topbaşı in Pera, then at Yeni Çarşı, and later in a shop at the Teké which we converted into a café. When I went to Crimea, he accompanied me with the aim of trading there. In short, we carried out all our business together.

Q. At what time did the said Andonaki go to Smyrna, and were you there at that time?
A. Since I have known him, I am not aware that he ever went to Smyrna.

Q. Do you know a certain Ludovico, an Austrian subject?
A. Yes, I know him.

Q. Since when and how did you know him?
A. For five years; he worked for three months in Andonaki’s carpentry workshop, that is when I came to know him.

Q. Did you associate with him afterward?
A. No. When we were at Varna, at Kamış, and at Kerch, he sometimes came to Andonaki’s hotel, my partner. He was associated with Diamanti; I saw him then, and later I encountered him in Constantinople, where we greeted each other without having any real relations.

Q. Since when have you known Roberto Diamanti?
A. I have known him for thirty years in my country, and when I arrived in Constantinople, I stayed at his house until the time he married. Later, before our departure for Crimea, we formed a partnership with him that lasted until our return.

Q. Was he your associate or Andonaki’s?
A. He was the associate of both of us.

Q. Did you have other associates?
A. Andonaki had one named Clitzi Cole.

Q. Were you part of the partnership that existed between Ludovico Boschi and Roberto?
A. No. I was partnered with Phylagone Andonaki, who left for India, and also with Ludovico and Roberto but he (Boschi) was not directly in partnership with me.

Q. Do you know anyone by the name of Raphael Boschi?
A. I had heard of the arrival at Kerch of an uncle of the said Boschi, but I had not seen him. Nor did I see him in Constantinople when I arrived. I do not know whether the individual you are asking about is the same person or another.

Q. What was the name of his uncle?
A. I never knew it.

Q. Have you known anyone by the name of Tomasso Facchini?
A. I have known no individual by that name.

Q. Or someone named Alexandro de Benetti?
A. No, I do not know him.

Q. Do you know Angelo Gennari?
A. Yes, I know him. We were on the same steamboat during my journey from Varna to Crimea, where we saw each other from time to time. I saw him again later more than once in Constantinople. The last time we met, near the Armenian church in Pera, he told me he was leaving for the island of Corfu and asked whether I would take charge of a commission he would give me. I replied that I would do so, provided it was feasible, and before his departure he gave me gold worth six hundred francs, along with a letter stating that the sum was intended for the purchase of items he would later order.

Q. Did he later order them from you? And what were these items?
A. While he was at Corfu, he wrote to me three or four times over the course of two months. In the first letter he asked for gloves; in the second, flower seeds; and in the third, marble. As I could not send him either the gloves or the seeds, I instructed my partner Andonaki to go and procure the marble himself, and upon his return I wrote to Angelo. However, a certain Ludovico Laurenzi, whom I do not know, brought me a letter from him and asked for the six hundred francs, which I handed over to him in exchange for a receipt that I kept along with the letter. I sent this receipt to Angelo Gennari enclosed in a letter I wrote to him. I have not received any letter from him since then, and although I wrote to him several times asking whether he had received the receipt, I received no reply. I had also responded to the two letters in which he had requested gloves and seeds, saying that I would take care of them, but I did not follow through because of their minor importance. In total, I wrote him only five or six letters.

Q. Does the letter you received from Angelo through Lorenzi still exist?
A. No, I lost it along with other papers I had.

Q. Through whom did you send your letter to Angelo Gennari?
A. I sent it by post addressed to Luigi Ergolani.

Q. Why did you send it to Luigi Ergolani instead of directly to Angelo?
A. I had known him in Switzerland under the name Luigi Ergolano, and I only later learned that his real name was Angelo Gennari. After he returned to Corfu, I continued to address my letters under the name by which I had originally known him.

Q. What signature was on the letters you received from Angelo Gennari?
A. They were signed Luigi Ergolano.

Q. Did you ever see Angelo Gennari with any of your acquaintances? Was his name ever mentioned in the course of their conversations?
A. I never met him with any of my acquaintances.

Q. How could you accept six hundred francs as a deposit from such a person, whom you had only known aboard a steamboat and whose name you did not know? And how could he entrust that sum to you without a receipt from you?
A. Since he had earned money through trade, and since he knew I was not the kind of man to cheat him out of his six hundred francs and perhaps he had obtained information about me from someone he trusted me.

Q. Do you know a certain Joseph, a Sardinian, Boschi?
A. I do not know him.

Q. Do you know his handwriting?
A. Yes, I know it.

Resumption of the interrogation of Gezri Venissi

Q. Where are the letters you received from Angelo Gennari, bearing the signature Luigi Ergolano?
A. I destroyed them.

Q. Do you remember the contents of the letters you wrote to Gennari?
A. I do not remember.

Q. Why did you continue to address the letters to Ergolano after you learned his true name?
A. He himself had instructed me to do so. Besides, I feared that if I did not put that name as the address, they would not reach their destination.

Q. The man you called Luigi Ergolano is not Angelo Gennari; why do you not tell the truth? A. As for me, I knew him under that name; perhaps later he adopted another one.

When he was made to read excerpts from the interrogation records drawn up in Bologna, passages indicating the participation of Gezri Venissi in the association, his equal partnership with the others, and his meeting with Raphael Boschi and Tomasso Facchini when they arrived in Constantinople to pass counterfeit papers, and also the depositions of Sebastiano Zaneti and Antonio Dareni, who had heard that Gezri Venissi was still involved in this affair, he declared that he did not know these individuals and that their statements against him were false. When he was again urged to confess the truth, he replied that he would submit to whatever might be done to him, but that if he persisted, it was because he could say nothing other than what he had already said.

Resumption of the interrogation of Gezri Venissi

Q. Do you intend to give a detailed account of the matter of which you are accused?
A. I have reflected during the two days you granted me, but I do not know what to say.

He was questioned again and urged to tell the truth. He replied that even if he had something to report, he would not be able to do so, adding that he had become involved in the matter by accident without participating in any way, and that he could not recount the facts without naming individuals; no matter how much they insisted, he would be unable to say anything.

Interrogation of Roberto Diamandi, Austrian subject

Q. What is your name and your father’s name? From what country are you? What is your nationality, and how old are you?
A. My name is Roberto Diamandi. My father’s name is Luigi. I am 36 years old. I am from Livorno and am an Austrian subject.

Q. Are you married or single? Where is your family?
A. I married six or seven years ago. My family is here, and I have a newborn child. My wife’s name is Anneta. She has two brothers: one is thirty years old and is named Francesco; the other is Giovanni Saterado.

Q. How long ago did you leave your country?
A. I left it six years ago and went to Constantinople.

Q. What is your profession, and why did you come to Constantinople?
A. I am a carpenter. I came here to practice my trade. After working for about three years in a workshop near the Teké in Pera, I went to Alexandria, where I also worked for six months. I then left and returned to my country, where I stayed for a year. From there I went to Marseille and Corsica, and then returned again to Constantinople.

I kept a workshop for four to five years at Yeni Çarşı in Pera and worked at the Sultan’s palace at Dolmabahçe Palace under the chief carpenter. But during the French expedition to Crimea, I gave up my shop and left for Crimea. I took with me Andonaki Draganicos, a Dutchman, my partner in the carpentry shop; he traded in spirits, wine, and other goods there on behalf of the partnership.

We stayed in Crimea for seven to eight months, then Andonaki and I returned to Constantinople. I left him there and went back again to Crimea. I made five or six trips there and back over the course of two and a half years during the war. After the war, I returned once more to Constantinople, where I have remained since, though I made three voyages to Galați, Foça, Sulina, and Burgaz, where I traded in coal.

Q. Did you have any associates other than Andonaki?
A. Upon my arrival in Pera, I formed a partnership with a certain Ludovico Boschi, with whom we engaged in trade; our partnership lasted six to seven months and was then dissolved.

Q. Where and under what circumstances did you meet Ludovico Boschi?
A. I met him fifteen days before my departure from Kamış.

Q. How long did your partnership with Andonaki last, and did you have other associates?
A. Our partnership lasted seven to eight years. However, when he was in Crimea, he was associated with a certain Baco Tezoli, and upon returning to Constantinople, he also worked with a man named Battia Gamalar, a French subject. As far as I know, he had no other associates.

Q. Were you ever associated with Gezri Venissi?
A. No.

Q. Did Andonaki have dealings with him?
A. I do not know.

Q. Do you know Gezri Venissi?
A. As he is my compatriot, I made his acquaintance seven years ago in Pera.

Q. Have you seen him since?
A. I have not seen him for three years.

Q. Did he ever lodge at your place?
A. Never.

Q. Were you in contact with Ludovico Boschi after your return from Crimea?
A. I had no dealings with him, except that we greeted each other when we met in the street.

Q. Do you know a Raphael Boschi?
A. I have never heard of such a person.

Q. Do you know an individual named Alexandre de Benti?
A. No.

Q. And a certain Tomasso Facchini?
A. No.

Q. Do you know why you are in prison?
A. When I was imprisoned, I was told it was because one of my friends had testified against me.

Q. Would the authorities have kept you in prison for four months on the testimony of a single friend?
A. That is what I see with my own eyes.

Q. What did your friend testify against you?
A. He said that I was a counterfeiter.

Q. According to the depositions and evidence against you, you were for a long time engaged with your associates in having counterfeit paper money produced elsewhere and then bringing it to be passed in Constantinople. Tell us the truth.
A. That is false.

Q. What will you say when the passages concerning you in the interrogation records drawn up in Bologna are read to you?
A. I could not believe in the existence of such statements even if they were made in my presence.

Q. Do you think perhaps that the persons interrogated did not make such statements? A. I do not doubt that they made the statements contained in those documents, but the names they cited are incorrect.

Resumption of the interrogation of Andonaki Draganico, English subject (who had been denied all communication with others)

Q. What is your name and your father’s name? What is your nationality? What country are you from? What is your age, and what is your profession?
A. My name is Andonaki Draganico; I am Ionian and an English subject. I am a carpenter and 32 years old.

Q. Are you married?
A. I am single.

Q. What have you done since arriving in Constantinople, and where were you established?
A. I have always practiced my trade since arriving in Constantinople. I worked for eight months in a workshop in Pera, near the Teké; then in Galata in the shop of a certain Prote; then another year in the shop of Giovanni Gapalino on the main street of Pera. I also worked at Tophane Başı with Calasso, a chief carpenter.

Later, I accompanied the allied armies to Varna and to Crimea, where I engaged in the trade of drinks and other goods. I then returned here and worked for more than a year for a certain Alord, a French carpenter established at Kulekapısı. After that, I opened a café in the same capacity, which I ran for two and a half years, and later sold it before my arrest.

Q. Were you associated with anyone when you left for Crimea?
A. Yes, we formed a partnership with Roberto Diamandi and Gezri Venissi, each contributing between 40,000 and 50,000 piastres of capital. My associates went to Crimea and conducted trade there, while I remained here working in carpentry with the workshop on behalf of the partnership. The said Roberto and Venissi returned after working for two years in Crimea, and after each of us had taken 100,000 piastres, we dissolved the partnership; however, we had not yet fully settled our accounts.

Venissi and I continued our business partnership and lived together.

Q. Were you associated with Venissi in business before your partnership in Crimea?
A. We lived together, but we were not partners before the Eastern War.

Q. And with Roberto? A. Yes, I was associated with him while I was working with Calasso, but not with Venissi; as the latter only arrived in Constantinople in 1851 or 1852, I met him through Roberto, and we admitted him among us.

Q. Did you know a certain Ludovico Boschi?
A. Yes. He was trading at Balaklava, and I did business with him for two months. He still owes me 1,200 piastres. After we were expelled from Balaklava by the military authorities, I asked him for my baptism certificate and my passports, which were in his trunk; but he, in turn, demanded from me twenty to thirty gold pounds that he claimed had been in it. A small dispute arose between us on that occasion, and since then we have not even greeted each other.

Q. How did your papers come to be in his trunk?
A. As I did not have a trunk myself and trusted him, I had placed them there.

Q. Why was he asking you for his trunk and his money?
A. We were lodging together in the same room at Balaklava; we were later expelled from that town without being allowed to take anything with us. Since he was then at Kamış, he later came to ask me for his chest.

Q. Do you know whether he had other associates at Balaklava?
A. I do not know.

Q. Do you know Raphael Boschi, his uncle, who had come to Balaklava?
A. No, I have never seen or known him.

Q. A Bolognese named Facchini arrived here, did you know him, as well as a certain Angelo Gennari?
A. No, I have neither known nor seen either of them.

Q. Did you know someone named Ergolano?
A. No.

Q. Did you go anywhere else after returning to Constantinople?
A. Yes. Having learned that a billiard table was being sought in Smyrna, I went there and, after staying a few days, returned here.

Q. Did you go anywhere else besides Smyrna? A. No, I did not go anywhere else. But I now recall that two or three months before my departure for Smyrna, having heard that a church was being built at Erdek, I planned to go there in the hope of finding work. However, as the construction of the church had not yet begun, I had to return to Constantinople after a month. On an order given to me by my associate Jefre, I picked up, on the way from Bandırma, four or five pieces of marble, which I brought back to him as samples.

Q. Why did Gezri Venissi ask you for this marble?
A. He did not tell me.

Q. Was it Venissi who sent you to Smyrna?
A. No, he was not even aware of my plan; I only informed him afterward.

Q. Which people did you associate with in Smyrna?
A. As I knew no one there, I associated with no one.

Q. Had you ever been to Smyrna before?
A. No, I had never been there.

Q. Did you see in that city Raphael Boschi, who was mentioned earlier?
A. I do not know that man, and I did not see him there.

Q. How long have you been in prison?
A. For about four months.

Q. Do you know the reason for your imprisonment?
A. I do not know.

Q. You, Ludovico Boschi who is here, Roberto Diamanti and his associate Gezri Venissi, and yourself, were passing counterfeit paper money here, which you had manufactured in Bologna through your associates in that city; and recently, when you were in Smyrna, you met Raphael Boschi, to whom you gave nine watches as his share in the proceeds of the counterfeit money he had previously brought you, and at the same time you entrusted him with two pieces of newly issued paper money to serve as models for the manufacture of further counterfeit notes—this is the reason for your imprisonment. What do you have to say?
A. I have no knowledge of any such counterfeiting.

Interrogation of Nito, a Latin subject

Q. What is your name and your father’s name? From what country are you, and what is your nationality?
A. My name is Pépito Galita, and my father’s name is Augustino. I am from Rome and an Ottoman subject.

Q. What is your profession? A. I am a painter.

Q. Do you practice your profession?
A. I am not very skilled in the trade, and I work when the opportunity arises.

Q. How old are you?
A. I am 44 years old.

Q. Do you know a certain Francesco de Petris, a Sardinian subject?
A. If I saw him, I might recognize him.

Q. Do you know Margarita, his wife?
A. Yes, I would recognize her if I saw her.

Q. This woman gave you counterfeit paper money, why do you not say so?
A. No, that is false.

Q. It is useless for you to deny it; you received counterfeit paper money from her, which you passed, and the police are aware of it.
A. That is false.

Q. Do you perhaps know a certain Antoni Plationiti?
A. Yes, I know him.

Q. On what occasion did you make his acquaintance?
A. I met him at the house of his father-in-law Batisti, when I used to go there.

Q. What relations did you have with Antoni?
A. We had no close relations; we only greeted each other when we met.

Q. Did Antoni ever pass counterfeit paper money?
A. I do not know.

Q. Antoni has stated, however, that you yourself passed such money, what do you say to that? A. He cannot say that.

Q. Do you think Margarita cannot say it either?
A. No; if they have said so, they must have been mistaken.

The said Nito, having been confronted with the woman Margarita, she stated that she had given him, over a period of eight to ten days, fifty pieces of 10-piastre paper money at the price of 2 1⁄2 piastres each. Nito persisted in his denial despite this statement by the said woman.

Antonaki, Gezri Venissi, Roberto Diamanti, and Ludovico Boschi all continued to completely deny the facts in their interrogations. However, since it resulted from the depositions of their other accomplices detained in Bologna that Raphael Boschi and Tomasso Facchini, in their earlier confessions at Constantinople, were in contact with these individuals, a confrontation was deemed necessary. But considering the impossibility of carrying it out here, the examining court decided and the Minister of Police ordered that photographic portraits be taken of the accused and sent to Bologna, in order to establish their identity by presenting them to the detained persons there who had denounced them.

For this purpose, the accused were sent on the 15th of Zilhidjé 1275 (Hijri year) to the photography establishment of Abdullah Frères, located at the Four Streets of Pera. Gezri Venissi and Roberto Diamanti allowed themselves to be photographed without difficulty, but Ludovico Boschi opposed it and made threats to the photographers, telling them that as long as his image remained on the plate, it would never be erased from his memory, thus justifying his refusal. He also told another photographer, who had been called in the meantime, that if money was the reason for wanting to photograph him, he could do without it and that he would pay him anyway, but that despite this he would still refuse. He added that, since he would soon regain his freedom, he would deal with the matter later. Therefore, no means remained to compel him to have his portrait taken. Moreover, he was excused from it, all the more so as his conduct in this matter was taken as evidence of his guilt.

Resumption of the interrogation of Roberto Diamandi

Q. Do you know Vincenzo Ribosaci?
A. Yes, I have known him for six or seven years. He is from Livorno, he is an architect, and he is about 35 years old. He is of standard height, dark complexion, with chestnut hair, thin, but I do not know where he lives.

Q. On what occasion did you meet him?
A. Since he is an architect and I am a carpenter, I had occasion to make his acquaintance.

Q. How long has it been since you last saw him?
A. Two years.

Q. Did you meet elsewhere? A. We saw each other at the Dardanelles.

Q. Did you have any relations with him?
A. I did, when we were in that city.,

Q. In connection with what matters were you in contact?
A. Concerning the construction of the British Consulate building in that city, which was under construction.

Q. Did you carry out the carpentry work on Mavrocordato’s house in Pera alone, or did you work there with someone else?
A. I worked there myself, with the help of a carpenter.

Q. Did you have any associate at that time?
A. Andonaki was my associate when I was working at Dolmabahçe Palace.

Q. Did Ribosaci take part in your business?
A. No.

Q. Did he have any relations with Andonaki?
A. As far as I know, he did not.

Q. In the letter Ribosaci wrote to you, he also sent greetings to him, how then can you be unaware of their connection with one another?
A. I do not know. It is possible that a connection exists between them, or that he sent his regards to Andonaki because of our friendship with him.

Q. You said that you knew Gezri Venissi, but that you had no friendly relations or dealings with him. However, it is known that in one of his letters he expressed friendship toward you.
A. I do not know whether the signature on that letter is that of Gezri Venissi. The handwriting of that letter does not resemble his.
