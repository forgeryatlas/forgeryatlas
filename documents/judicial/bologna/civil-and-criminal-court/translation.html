No. 157 — 1860
Civil and Criminal Court of First Instance in Bologna

No. 148 Quer.

BOLOGNA

In the Case of Counterfeiting and Circulation of Turkish Banknotes

For the Curia and the Treasury

Against

Raffaele Randaboschi, known as Boschi, son of the late Luigi, aged 40, native of Budrio, married with children, residing in Bologna, cook and café owner, arrested on February 1, 1858.

Raffaele Crudeli, son of the late Domenico Martino, aged 40, from Bologna, and singer — arrested between February 27 and 28 of the said year.

Tommaso Facchini, son of Celestino, aged 50, married with children, from Bologna, landowner — arrested between February 27 and 28 of the said year.

Sebastiano Zanetti, son of Vincenzo, known as Bastiano and Luigi, aged 54, from Bologna, married with children, lithographic printer — arrested between February 25 and 26 of the said year.

Antonio Dareni, son of Gaetano, aged 27, from Bologna, married with children, lithographic printer — arrested between February 25 and 26 of the said year.

Enrico Corty, son of the late Cristiano, aged 51, born in Schwerin in Mecklenburg, residing in Bologna, married with children, lithographic printer — arrested on March 9, 1859.

Guglielmo Thumb, son of Carlo, aged 22, native of Württemberg in Germany, residing in Bologna, unmarried, lithographic engraver — arrested on March 10 of the said year.

Vincenzo Busi — absconding (tried in absentia).

Relation

When the war in Crimea was at its height, and specifically in the summer of 1855, Raffaele Randaboschi went there to assist his nephew Lodovico Randaboschi, known as Boschi, who had opened a provisions shop for the benefit of the troops. Meanwhile, together with Lodovico, they conceived the plan of counterfeiting Ottoman banknotes; and when Raffaele returned to Bologna toward the end of the summer of 1856, he spoke of it to Raffaele Crudeli and Tommaso Facchini. These agreed to the plan, and in order to achieve their aim they formed among themselves a partnership, in which expenses and profits were to be shared.

They took into their service as a mechanic Enrico Corty, a lithographic printer, who undertook to imitate the 20-piastre note, corresponding in value to four francs, copying a genuine one that Raffaele Randaboschi had brought back from Turkey as a model. For this purpose, Corty, with funds supplied to him by Crudeli and Facchini, prepared the necessary materials, namely lithographic stones, stamps, and a press, and in fact printed five to six thousand notes of that type.

Corty therefore worked for about six months in a room assigned to him in Crudeli’s house, receiving meanwhile a monthly payment of nine or ten scudi, and later he was to receive a share of the profits equal to that of the other partners. Facchini then sent to Lodovico Boschi in Constantinople six hundred of these notes, concealed in a box of toothpicks, so that they might be put into circulation. However, because Boschi did not remit money, and because disagreements arose between Crudeli and Facchini concerning the funds they had respectively advanced to meet expenses, the partnership was dissolved, and most of the counterfeit notes and the materials used in their production were destroyed.

Raffaele Randaboschi, however, together with Facchini, persistent in their purpose, formed another partnership for the same objective toward the end of 1857 and the beginning of 1858. To this new association belonged Vincenzo Busi and Sebastiano Zanetti, a lithographic printer, as well as Alessandro Donnini and Lodovico Randaboschi, who were in Constantinople and who in turn came to Bologna; also Roberto Diamanti, Vincenzo Archibugiani, Cesare Venzi, and Cesare Antonucci, likewise residing in Constantinople. Among all these, the profits to be obtained were to be shared.

In this new partnership, the mechanic was Sebastiano Zanetti, along with the aforementioned Corty. Zanetti prepared the lithographic stone and the press for printing the twenty-piastre notes, while Corty made the two required stamps, one for dry use and the other for use with ink. The work was carried out in Facchini’s house, and Sebastiano Zanetti was the one who printed six to seven thousand notes of the said type, assisted also by Antonio Dareni, a lithographic printer and his brother-in-law.

Once this work was completed, Facchini, together with Donnini, who was temporarily in Bologna, and Raffaele Randaboschi departed for Constantinople. There, having transported the said notes divided among their trunks, they delivered them to their associates for distribution; and Facchini transferred them to Donnini, from whom he received five hundred Turkish piastres. From Constantinople, Facchini went on to France, and when he returned home, reflecting that the profit was small and the responsibility great, he withdrew from the enterprise and refused further involvement.

Raffaele Randaboschi, however, along with Zanetti and Busi, did not desist, and in order to better succeed in their undertaking they enlisted the engraver Guglielmo Thumb, who prepared additional lithographic stones to print not only the 20-piastre notes but also those of 10 piastres. At that time Zanetti worked in his own house, and the aforementioned Antonio Dareni continued to assist him.

Altogether, more than twelve thousand notes of these two types were produced, and these were gradually sent to Constantinople and put into circulation. Eventually, Raffaele Randaboschi and Zanetti, together with their associates in Constantinople, also resolved to counterfeit another 20-piastre note that had recently been issued there. For this purpose, Lodovico Randaboschi had sent two samples to Zanetti, and Raffaele had brought two more with him upon his return from Turkey.

To achieve this aim, the necessary machine for producing the wavy patterns and outlines had already been purchased, and the engraver Thumb, at Zanetti’s request, had prepared the required lithographic stones also for this latest type of note. Zanetti had already printed two of these notes and had sent them to Lodovico Randaboschi in Constantinople so that he might examine them and give his opinion, namely, whether, once the design had been perfected, the notes would be suitable for circulation.

Meanwhile, the Ottoman police took notice of Lodovico Randaboschi, arrested him, and found in his possession two of the said counterfeit 10-piastre notes, as well as one of the new 20-piastre notes likewise forged and sent to him by Zanetti, as previously mentioned.

Having thus obtained the thread of the matter, the Sublime Porte began to unravel it, and having discovered that the manufacture was being carried out in Bologna, it sent there, through legal channels, an official representative. With the information thus supplied to the local Police Directorate, the perpetrators of the said crime were identified and arrested. When duly interrogated, the accused did not hesitate to confess their guilt in the manner already described.

Raffaele Crudeli stated that in the summer of 1856, when Raffaele Randaboschi returned from Constantinople, whom he already knew, he went to visit him; Randaboschi showed him Turkish coins and banknotes and revealed his plan to imitate them. He agreed to the scheme and, being a friend of Facchini, informed him of it, proposing that he also join them, which he did. At first they had intended to counterfeit coin, but the difficulties proved insurmountable, and they therefore resolved to counterfeit only paper money, which they in fact carried out in the manner and with the means already described.

Facchini likewise admitted that, according to the arrangements made with Crudeli and Raffaele Randaboschi, and with the cooperation of Corty, they prepared the said notes in imitation of the Ottoman 20-piastre currency, and that he himself sent six hundred of them to Lodovico Boschi in Constantinople to be put into circulation. Continuing his confession, he added that later Crudeli withdrew, and that he, together with the other associates already mentioned, and with the materials and means described, produced in his own house another six to seven thousand notes of the same denomination. These Raffaele Randaboschi took with Donnini to Constantinople in the manner already indicated, and once they arrived there, he handed over those he had in his trunk to Donnini, from whom he received five hundred Turkish piastres to cover travel expenses.

Corty, in every respect, confirmed the foregoing account in his confession, thus corroborating what Facchini and Crudeli had stated. Speaking of the first partnership, he said that he alone carried out the work, printing five to six thousand 20-piastre notes in Crudeli’s house. As for the second partnership, he stated that at Facchini’s request he made the two stamps, for which Facchini paid him ten scudi; he added that he had agreed to undertake the work out of necessity, being in extreme poverty and needing to earn his bread.

Raffaele Randaboschi also confessed his serious responsibility in the events described. He stated that, having returned from Constantinople toward the end of the summer of 1856, he reached an agreement with Crudeli, Facchini, and Corty for the production of the said notes, which were indeed counterfeited in the quantity already indicated. He added that, since this first partnership had little success, a second one was formed, to which likewise belonged Facchini, Zanetti, Busi, and Donnini, as well as the others residing in Constantinople. Within this second partnership, another quantity of Ottoman 20-piastre notes was counterfeited in Facchini’s house and then transported by himself, Donnini, and Facchini to Constantinople in the manner already described.

He acknowledged that Facchini later withdrew from the partnership, while he himself continued, together with the others, in counterfeiting the said 20-piastre notes, as well as those of 10 piastres, with Zanetti working in his own house. He further stated that they had resolved to imitate the 20-piastre note that had recently been put into circulation in Turkey, and that for this purpose he had purchased from the lithographer Mr. Venck a machine for 400 scudi, the money having been sent to him by the associates in Constantinople. The lithographic stones had also been prepared, and the partner Zanetti, using some samples sent to him by his nephew Lodovico and others delivered by himself, had already printed a few of these notes and had sent them to Lodovico so that he might examine whether, once perfected, the work would be usable. Finally, he declared that he had received from the associates in Constantinople, in payment for the notes put into circulation, various sums of money, as well as nine watches from Antonacci—one of which he gave to Sebastiano Zanetti, another to Dareni, and another he kept for himself, later selling six of them for 52 scudi to the watchmaker Angelo Benassi.

Sebastiano Zanetti, in his confession, substantially confirmed what had been set forth in the narrative, and the other accomplices also corroborated it; therefore it would be unnecessary to repeat the details here. It is only worth noting that he also confessed to having obtained, during the partnership, as his share, 41 gold coins of 20 francs each. Of these, however, he had to spend 15 to pay Thumb for the lithographic stones he had prepared, giving him precisely nine napoleons and 25 paoli, and also a watch valued at ten scudi, diverting another small portion to compensate his brother-in-law Dareni, who, as mentioned, assisted him in preparing the work.

Antonio Dareni likewise confessed to having rendered his services to his brother-in-law Zanetti, both in Facchini’s house and in their own home, where they lived together; but he claimed to have done so only to oblige Zanetti, to whom he felt indebted. He further confessed that Zanetti had once given him ten scudi, and on another occasion a gold napoleon, and that Raffaele Randaboschi, returning from Constantinople, had given him a watch. However, he maintained that he had other grounds, independent of his material participation in the manufacture of the notes, for receiving these payments—an assertion for which he provided no justification.

Finally, Guglielmo Thumb also confessed, like all the others, and stated that at Zanetti’s request he made the aforementioned lithographic stones, believing, however, that it was not a matter of counterfeiting Ottoman banknotes, but only of printing labels to be affixed to packages of Turkish tobacco, as Zanetti had assured him. This claim, however, appears implausible if one considers that the notes were furnished with seals, that there were impressions on both sides, and that this is incompatible with the supposed use; and furthermore if one considers that Zanetti insisted on strict secrecy in carrying out the work, which Thumb scrupulously observed.

Nevertheless, in defense of the said accused Thumb, it may be taken into account that the accomplices Zanetti and Dareni maintain that Thumb worked in good faith, because they had assured him that the items were tobacco labels; and as regards the secrecy, it should be noted that, being employed by the lithographers De Maria and Venck, he had an interest in concealing that he was working for outsiders and outside their establishments.

To verify the aforementioned confessions, the following prosecutorial evidence is found in the proceedings. The lawful seizure from the accused Crudeli of a lithographic stone, which had been used to print the banknotes while work was being carried out in his house, and which the accused himself recognized as such; moreover, he also identified the press indicated by Corty, which had likewise been used on that occasion.

The fact that the aforementioned Corty recognized the said stone seized from Crudeli, as well as the press that had been in his possession and had served for the aforesaid manufacture; and likewise that Corty identified the two stamps he had made at the request of Facchini, which were seized from Sebastiano Zanetti and had been used by the second partnership for the imitation of the notes in Facchini’s house.

The fact that the accomplice Facchini also recognized the said press, which had been operated in Crudeli’s house, as well as one of the stamps made by Corty for the use of the second partnership when work was carried out in his own house; he excused himself, however, from identifying the remaining tools, stating that he did not have a precise recollection of them.

The fact that all the materials used for the counterfeiting of the said notes were lawfully seized from Sebastiano Zanetti, and recognized by him as such, namely, the four lithographic stones, the two seals, the press, the machine, and the other printing implements; as well as four 20-piastre notes each, of recent circulation, which served him as models for imitating the new issue; and that he likewise recognized the two counterfeit 10-piastre notes taken from Lodovico Boschi at the time of his arrest in Constantinople, and the newly issued 20-piastre note seized from the said Boschi.

The fact that the accused Raffaele Randaboschi also legally recognized the lithographic stone seized from Crudeli, as well as the other four taken from the accomplice Zanetti, together with the seals, the machine purchased from the lithographer Wenck, the 20-piastre notes of recent circulation used as models, the two counterfeit 10-piastre notes seized from his nephew Lodovico when he was arrested in Constantinople, as mentioned above, and finally the watches he had received from Antonucci in exchange for the large quantity of notes, most of which he then passed on to the watchmaker Angelo Benassi.

The fact that the accomplice Antonio Dareni likewise recognized all the materials that had been used both in Facchini’s house and in the house of his brother-in-law Sebastiano Zanetti, where the notes were produced and where he himself took part in the work.

The fact that the mechanic Guglielmo Thumb also recognized the lithographic stones seized from Zanetti as those on which he himself had drawn and engraved the 10- and 20-piastre notes of the old circulation, as well as the new 20-piastre notes, and that he also identified two samples of the said new notes among those that Zanetti had given him to copy.

The testimony of the lithographer Mr. Giulio Wenck, who stated that he had sold to the accused Raffaele Randaboschi, about three months earlier, for the price of 100 scudi, the aforementioned machine suitable for producing wave patterns and relief effects, Randaboschi having led him to believe that he was purchasing it for the use of a lithographer in Padua.

The testimony of the other lithographer, Innocenzo Demaria, who confirmed that, at Crudeli’s request and merely as a loan, he entrusted him with one of his lithographic stones, Crudeli having claimed that he intended to engrave it with the signs of the Zodiac. The testimony of Angelo Benassi, who stated that in the past autumn he had purchased from Raffaele Randaboschi the aforementioned watches, and who also proceeded to identify all those that were recovered.

The testimony of the shipping agent Luigi Meni, who stated that in the past months of November and January he had in fact sent to Constantinople two crates containing grease, as the accused Raffaele Randaboschi confessed, revealing that within those same crates several thousand counterfeit notes had been concealed. The state of poverty of some of the accused, and in others the desire for gain, as their affairs were somewhat disordered, may have been the motives for the crime.

Finally, it must not be omitted that the expert lithographic examination clearly demonstrated that all the materials seized from Sebastiano Zanetti, namely lithographic stones, stamps, an iron machine, a press, together with other printing tools, were perfectly suited for the imitation of genuine Ottoman paper currency of all three indicated types, as recorded in the proceedings on pages 500–501–502; and that the two counterfeit 10-piastre notes likewise included on pages 142–143 were highly capable of deceiving and had been printed with one of the aforementioned lithographic stones constituting the corpus delicti; whereas the 20-piastre note of the new issue, referred to on page 144, is incomplete and therefore cannot and must not be considered capable of deceiving anyone.

Thus the final conclusion was reached against all the present accused, who remained firm in their confessions, with the sole exception that the defendant Facchini claimed that in counterfeiting the said notes he did not believe he was committing an unlawful act, condemned and punishable by law; and with regard to the fugitive Vincenzo Busi, proceedings in absentia were undertaken.

Bologna, 31 October 1860.

N. ZACCARI, examining magistrate.
