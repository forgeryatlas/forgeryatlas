Bologna, 19 April 1861
Public Prosecutor General at the Court of Appeal of Bologna

Rustem Bey, Chargé d’Affaires of Turkey (signed)

Sir Chargé d’Affaires,

Since the proceedings concerning the manufacture of counterfeit kaimes have not only been opened but have also been actively pursued and directed, from the outset, with great diligence by a special delegate of your government, it has been assumed that it was your intention to appear as a civil party—such constitution being permitted, under our legislation, at any stage up to the close of the hearings.

Moreover, even in your capacity solely as an injured party, it was appropriate to inform you of the date set for the hearing of the case, so that you might assess whether it would be in the interest of your government to take part. It was from this perspective that I hastened to notify you, in my dispatch of the 6th of the current month, that the hearings would take place shortly and that you would be informed of the exact date, which has now been fixed for the first day of next May.

However, this notice, which will also be conveyed to you through diplomatic channels, does not in any way oblige you to assume the status of civil party, if you do not consider it to be in the interest or in keeping with the dignity of your government, whose high and noble mission you are entrusted to safeguard—a mission you fulfill in such a worthy and honorable manner. This, I repeat, is entirely optional for you up to the close of the hearings, that is, until the 1st of next month; it is in no way obligatory, not even to appear at the hearings.

As to the outcome of the case, you are fully justified in relying on the integrity, impartiality, and firmness of our judiciary. It is and will always be equal to its duties. The facts, moreover, regarding the manufacture of counterfeit Ottoman paper money, have been admitted by the accused. Thus, from this point of view, there can be no doubt about a verdict of guilt.

However, I will not conceal from you that a rather serious legal issue may arise, namely that the manufacture of counterfeit foreign paper money was not punishable, nor even provided for, under the penal laws that were in force under the Papal government. My office will make every effort to ensure that this issue, once raised, is resolved in favor of the prosecution. It will not fail to develop and emphasize the various arguments supporting its position, particularly the duty of governments to cooperate in ensuring that public trust is respected, even with regard to other states, and to stress that, taken as a whole, the provisions of the penal law in force in 1858 clearly show that it was the spirit of the law and the intention of the legislator to repress all forms of counterfeiting.

But, I repeat, so that you may judge what it is appropriate for you to do, the matter is not without difficulty.

Please accept, Sir Chargé d’Affaires, the assurances of my highest consideration.

The Prosecutor General (signed) Bianchi
