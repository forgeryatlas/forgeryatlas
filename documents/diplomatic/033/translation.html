To Rüstem Bey, Chargé d’Affaires of Turkey, etc., Turin
Turin, 24 January 1859

Sir Chargé d’Affaires,

Having just received from the Department of Justice detailed information on the course and outcome of the proceedings instituted by the Sardinian judicial authority against the individual named Ambroise Bondesio, under the charge of complicity in the manufacture, on Sardinian territory, of counterfeit Turkish paper money, I hasten to reply to the communication which you addressed to me on this subject, dated the 15th of the current month.

I consider it appropriate to transmit to you for this purpose the attached copy of a report by the Attorney General of Turin, which gives a detailed account of the case, and from the contents of which you will be able to learn the reasons for the decision of dismissal (non-lieu) rendered by the Tribunal with regard to Bondesio.

You will also note, Sir Chargé d’Affaires, that since the proceedings against Bondesio have always been separate and distinct from those still pending against the individuals named Varallo Pandolfini, Venanzi, and others, the intervention of the representative of the Sublime Porte, as a civil party in the proceedings against the latter, could not be regarded as implying an intervention of the same nature in the proceedings against Bondesio, for which it would also have been necessary to comply with the formalities prescribed by Article 42 of the Code of Criminal Procedure. It is for this reason that it was not deemed necessary to notify you of the dismissal decision rendered in favor of Bondesio; and that the absence of such notification might perhaps be useful to you in order to lodge an objection to the said decision, should you consider it appropriate. However, in any case, such an objection would very probably be rejected, since during the course of the proceedings it was in no way established that Bondesio had taken part in the manufacture of counterfeit caimés within the King’s States.

I hasten further to add, in response to an observation contained in your aforementioned communication, that Article 6 of the Sardinian Penal Code does indeed provide that crimes committed abroad by a Sardinian subject shall be judged and punished in accordance with the provisions of that same code; however, this provision could not be applied to Bondesio in this instance, since, according to Articles 335 and 347, in order for the counterfeiting of coin or public instruments of a foreign country to be considered punishable under our laws, it is necessary—indeed I repeat—that it be committed on Sardinian territory.

I must also point out to you, with regard to the four interrogations whose translations were attached to your aforementioned communication, that the first two—namely those of Baptiste Buonjudy and Aristide Ballatiano—were communicated in due time to the Sardinian judicial authority, which found in them confirmation of the indications concerning Bondesio’s complicity in the manufacture of counterfeit caimes at Constantinople; but found nothing therein that could establish him as an accomplice in a similar falsification committed at Turin. As for the other two interrogations, namely those of Pedrozzi and Calvocoressi, they contain, with regard to Bondesio, only evasive or negative answers. These documents therefore cannot in any way alter the state of the case with which we are concerned.

I trust, Sir Chargé d’Affaires, that upon careful examination of the attached report, you will be convinced of the perfect regularity of the proceedings of the Sardinian judicial authority, and of the impossibility in which the King’s Government finds itself to grant the request which you addressed to me at the conclusion of your aforementioned communication. For it is not within its power to annul or to revise judgments rendered by the competent courts in accordance with the law; on the contrary, it must itself respect them and require their observance. And whatever its desire may be to demonstrate on all occasions the sentiments of sincere friendship which animate it toward the Ottoman Government, it cannot do so at the cost of committing an arbitrary act in direct opposition to the decisions of justice.

Please accept, Sir Chargé d’Affaires, the renewed assurances of my highest consideration.

(signed) C. Cavour
