Turin, November 17, 1859
No. 628

Monseigneur,

Immediately upon receiving the dispatch which Your Excellency did me the honor of sending me on the 3rd of this month (No. 3359), concerning the proceedings initiated against the individuals imprisoned in Bologna under the charge of manufacturing counterfeit caimes, I hastened, in accordance with Your Excellency’s instructions, to put myself in communication with my colleague in Naples in order to consult with him on the measures to be taken to bring about a prompt resolution of this case, which has been dragging on for so long.

Spitzer Effendi has sent me a copy of a dispatch which Your Excellency addressed to him on July 20, 1859 (No. 2789). He further promises to request from Mr. Giri the extracts from the Penal Code and the three photographs mentioned in Your Excellency’s dispatch, and to forward them to me as soon as he receives them.

Allow me to observe to Your Excellency that these documents are by no means sufficient to bring the proceedings to a successful conclusion. Your Excellency’s dispatch is only a summary of a report from the Ministry of Police. Now, Your Excellency cannot be unaware that courts in Europe accept police magistrates’ reports only as acts of accusation and not as evidence for the prosecution.

I therefore find myself obliged to request once again, in the most urgent manner, that Your Excellency be pleased to insist with the Ministry of Police that the following be provided as soon as possible:

1° At least one of the counterfeit caimes transmitted by Thomas Facchini of Bologna to Ludovico Randaboschi, known as Boschi, in Constantinople. The caime must be accompanied by a duly legalized certificate attesting to its origin and its falsity. It is probable that Mr. Tkiadès had already delivered such items to the Police of Bologna; however, owing to the political events that occurred in that city, the Police Directorate withdrew to Rome, and these caimes were probably carried off or even, possibly, someone may have taken advantage of the inevitable disorder caused by such serious political upheavals to make them disappear, thus destroying the corpus delicti and attempting to prevent a conviction. It is therefore very important to send others.

2° The portfolio which, having been stolen from Facchini while he was in Constantinople, fell into the hands of the police of that capital; this portfolio must likewise be accompanied by a duly legalized certificate from the Police of Constantinople attesting to its origin.

3° A legal document from the Ministry of Police of Constantinople establishing the falsity and the circulation of the caimes referred to above.

4° Certified translations of the interrogations of the accused arrested in Constantinople, as well as those conducted previously in the case of G. B. Bongiacci and his associates, who were sentenced under the former Penal Code to three years of forced labor.

I urgently request Your Excellency to insist with the Ministry of Police that a clear and definitive reply be given without further delay on each of these four points. Moreover, in order to anticipate the possibility that Mr. Giri, former Director of Police of Bologna, now a refugee in Rome, and to whom the Ottoman Chargé d’Affaires in Naples has delivered the translation sent by Your Excellency of the articles concerning counterfeiters—drawn both from the former Penal Code and from that now in force—might raise difficulties in returning these documents, I consider it necessary to request that Your Excellency kindly have a duly legalized copy of these translations sent to me.

Your Excellency will recognize, I have no doubt, the harmful effect that the acquittal of the accused presently detained in Bologna would produce, and how important it is to secure their conviction as a warning to those who might be tempted to follow such a criminal example. Your Excellency will therefore understand the importance I attach to being able, as soon as possible, to supply the authorities of Bologna with all the documents they require and thus remove any pretext for the acquittal of the accused.

I have the honor to be, Monseigneur, Your Excellency’s most humble and most obedient servant,

Rüstem
