Rüstem Bey Chargé d’Affaires of Turkey in Turin
Turin, 5 March 1859

Sir,

In accordance with the instructions you were good enough to give me, I attended the hearings of the trial of the individuals Varallo Pandolfini and Venanzi, detained in Turin, and Spadafora and Mayner Veiller, tried in absentia, accused of manufacturing counterfeit caimes (Ottoman paper currency). The hearings took place before the Court of Appeal of Turin on 28 February and 1 March. I now have the honor to give you a brief account of the incidents and pleadings to which this trial gave rise, as well as of the judgment of the Court which concluded the first phase of the proceedings. 

After the reading of the indictment and the declaration of default against Spadafora and Mayner Veiller, the Court first had to deliberate on two objections raised by the defense. One, raised on behalf of Venanzi, requested that the Court declare itself incompetent to judge him, since he was a Roman subject accused of an act that constituted a crime only in Turkey. Varallo’s defense, for its part, contested the legitimacy of the civil claim, denying the representative of the Sublime Porte the right and authority to seek, abroad, reparation for a wrong committed in the Ottoman Empire.

After hearing the response of the counsel for the civil party, the Court rejected the plea of incompetence and declared the civil action entirely legitimate, recognizing the Turkish chargé d’affaires as the natural representative of all the rights and interests of the Government of the Sublime Porte.

During their interrogations, the accused attempted to mislead the judges through statements contradicting their earlier confessions. Venanzi was sharply reprimanded when he tried to justify his participation in this criminal enterprise by citing the example of similar operations and the impunity they enjoyed under what he described as a government as bad as that of Turkey. Both Venanzi and Varallo, in order to exonerate themselves, shifted all responsibility onto Spadafora, Mayner, and Calvocoressi, whom they described as the founders and directors of the operation, while presenting themselves merely as unskilled workers.

The counsel for the civil party then presented a claim for damages. Based on calculations derived from the number of notes produced using the missing paper, the quantity still in existence, and the number of notes seized, the amount was estimated at 37,804 francs, representing an approximate and probable total value in France of the counterfeit caimes circulated by the association to which the accused belonged in the Ottoman Empire. The lawyer concluded by declaring, in the name of the representative of the Sublime Porte, that this sum would be donated to the charitable institution (Mendicity Establishment) of Turin.

The Public Prosecutor then delivered his submissions and, after demonstrating the guilt of the accused, requested that all be sentenced to 15 years of hard labor.

Venanzi’s defense argued that the only article of Sardinian law punishing the manufacture of paper money applied exclusively to currency circulating within the Royal States; since caimes did not fall into that category, the counterfeiting of such notes was not considered a crime under Sardinian law. Therefore, the silence of the law should prevail over any arbitrary interpretation of the cited article. He supported this argument with references to several legal authorities and concluded his lengthy plea by expressing surprise at the severity of the penalty sought by the prosecution, attributing it to political influence—an influence, he argued, which the Court should disregard. He requested Venanzi’s acquittal or, at most, the lesser penalty applicable to accomplices.

Varallo’s defense first declared full adherence to the arguments made on behalf of Venanzi, then proceeded with a series of denials which ultimately revealed the weakness of his case. In attempting to deny Varallo’s voluntary complicity—particularly regarding the application of the engraved stamp on the caimes produced in Turin—he prompted the Court to examine Varallo’s correspondence, accounts, and even banknotes clearly bearing the stamp. He further claimed that the Sublime Porte had suffered no damage, since it had not been proven that the counterfeit notes had actually been circulated in Turkey.

The Court then withdrew to deliberate in private. After two hours, it returned and pronounced its judgment:

Varallo Pandolfini and Venanzi (present), together with Spadafora and Mayner Veiller (in absentia), were found guilty of the crime of manufacturing counterfeit paper money to the detriment of the Ottoman Government and were sentenced to eight years of imprisonment.

With regard to the claim for damages by the civil party, the Court held that no precise data existed to allow an exact calculation of the amount of counterfeit caimes circulated in Turkey. It therefore referred the civil party to the competent tribunal, which would have to recalculate and determine the appropriate compensation to be recovered from the accused.

In concluding this brief report, I consider it my duty to inform you that the accused intend to appeal the judgment. The Court of Cassation must render its decision within forty days, and should it (however unlikely) overturn the ruling of the Court of Appeal of Turin, the case would be referred to another appellate court within the Royal States for a new trial against Venanzi and Varallo.

Please accept, Sir, the assurances of my highest consideration.

(Signed)
