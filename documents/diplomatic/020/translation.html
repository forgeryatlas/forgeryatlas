In the Name of His Majesty Victor Emmanuel II

Order of the Chamber of Council at the Royal Tribunal of Turin

Against:

Alexandre Venanzi, son of the late Michel Ange, aged 26, born in Rome and recently residing in this capital, by profession a sculptor and metal founder.

Louis Varallo Pandolfini, son of the late Simon, aged 41, born in Moncalvo and residing in this capital.

Philippe Spadafora, known as Peppo, engraver on copper and stone, native of Rome, having recently resided in this capital, aged 36.

Augustin Veiller Mayner, lithographer and dealer in engravings, native of Rome, who appears to have resided for some time in Turin, about 50 years old.

Dimitri (Demetrius) Calvocoressi, calling himself in this capital Mircovich, of Greek origin, residing in Constantinople, having temporarily resided in this capital.

The first two are in custody; the others are absent.

Charged with:

the counterfeiting of foreign paper currency, for having, in the last months of the year 1857 or at the beginning of the current year, in this city and in a house situated in the district of the Madonna del Pilone, jointly engaged in the manufacture, to the prejudice of the Sublime Porte, of the public credit paper of that Empire, issued under the name of caimes (Ottoman paper money), each of the value of 20 piastres, producing up to 17,701 notes corresponding to 354,020 piastres, a large portion of which is said to have been exported from these Royal States.

Having regard to the conclusions of the prosecution of March 31 last;

Considering that the proof of the crime in question emerges beyond doubt:

From the seizure carried out at Varallo’s residence of the press, tools, and papers intended for the manufacture, as well as an impression of a forged note;

From the subsequent discovery, in the garden adjoining that residence, of a sack containing a large quantity of counterfeit notes, some already bearing the necessary seals, along with analogous transparent designs and certain notes containing the fictitious accounts of the counterfeiters;

From the numerous testimonies which clearly prove their association, and their repeated secret meetings held with caution in a room specially intended for this criminal manufacture;

From the established fact that the accused, and particularly Calvocoressi, commissioned from the firm Avando a quantity of paper intended for the said manufacture, as evidenced by a corresponding agreement attached to the order, in which Calvocoressi falsely signed under the name of Mircovich, to which agreement is also annexed a sample of the commissioned paper, consisting of a small piece taken from a genuine caime

Finally, from the explicit declarations of Venanzi and Varallo, from which it becomes known that the plan to carry out this criminal manufacture had been established in Constantinople.

These proofs further strengthen the information gathered by the Ottoman authorities during the interrogation of the detainees in Constantinople, namely Calvocoressi and his accomplice, the lawyer Marcel Brescianini of Brescia.

There is no reason for the Chamber of Council to concern itself with the latter, since he is not a Sardinian subject and it has not been established that he entered these Royal States with the other accomplices, despite the correspondence and connections he may have had with them.

As for Antoinette Biancardi, since no evidence has emerged from the investigation proving that this woman, who was presented as Varallo’s wife, can be considered an accomplice in the crime in question, it is ordered that she be released from the prison in which she is held.

Orders that the case be transmitted to the office of the Attorney General for matters concerning Venanzi, Varallo, Spadafora, Mayner, and Calvocoressi.

Turin, April 1, 1858

(Signed) Mattei, President – Druetti and Simi, Judges

Countersigned: Serracca, Deputy Fiscal Advocate, and Costa, Secretary

On the thirty-first day of March 1858, in Turin, in the criminal prisons.

Before us, Charles Druetti, lawyer, judge acting as examining magistrate, with the assistance of the undersigned deputy secretary,

Following the transmission made to us of the translation of the interrogations conducted in Constantinople of the lawyer Brescianini and of Calvocoressi, it appears appropriate to question again the individuals detained in these criminal prisons, although the substance forming the subject of these interrogations has already been addressed in the last interrogation of Varallo Pandolfini; and having first called the said Louis Varallo, son of the late Simon, he confirmed what he had stated in his previous interrogation recorded in the statement of March 24 of the current month.

Being again urged to declare whether among the accomplices in the crime in question there was not an individual with the given name Alphonse and the family name Innocent, Simon, or Agnor, and particularly whether he might not be of French origin:

Answer:
I can affirm that no Alphonse is known to me in connection with the manufacturer in question, and as for the names Innocent, Simon, and Agnor, I cannot even imagine to whom these names might belong.

Question:
Whether he has ever been on the French steamer Algérie with Calvocoressi?

Answer:
I have never been with Calvocoressi except at Galata, in Constantinople, and in Turin.

Question:
Whether on that steamer a quantity of counterfeit Turkish banknotes was not consigned to Calvocoressi?

Answer:
No, Sir.

Question:
Whether, upon arriving in Turin, Calvocoressi did not deliver letters written by Brescianini to Louis Varallo and Alexandre Venanzi?

Answer:
Finding myself one day with Calvocoressi and Spadafora, the latter said to me: “Has Calvocoressi not read Brescianini’s letter?” I replied no. Then Calvocoressi searched in his pocket and did not find the letter, which, as I then understood, had been communicated both to Spadafora and to Venanzi. I asked what it contained. They answered: the usual phrases of Brescianini, and I learned no more because I did not see the letter. It is a fact that Calvocoressi did not give me any letter.

Question:
Whether he is aware that in Constantinople some Neapolitan subjects undertook the manufacture of counterfeit Turkish paper money; and whether he also knows that in certain cities of France this criminal manufacture was likewise undertaken?

Answer:
On this subject I cannot give any precise information, limiting myself to saying that in Constantinople it is well known that the criminal manufacture in question is carried on beyond all belief, and that Brescianini was in contact with a very large number of persons reputed to manufacture the paper in question and even more; but as for precise information useful for further investigations, I am not in a position to provide any.

Subsequently, Varallo was subjected to all the most detailed interrogations that could be required in light of the translations that had been transmitted to us, as mentioned above, but it was not possible for the office to obtain any further clarification.

After reading and confirmation, the detainee signed together with the office; the detainee observing that, according to the facts he had reported, particularly in his previous interrogation, he is firmly convinced that Calvocoressi came to Piedmont for the manufacture in question at the instigation of Brescianini.

Original: Louis Varallo-Pandolfini

(Signed) Druetti and Manfredi, Deputy Secretary

Subsequently, the individual named Alexandre Venanzi was brought into the interrogation room and questioned on all the circumstances on which he had already been examined.

He replied: It is true that I had relations with Brescianini; indeed, I would say that I had only a simple acquaintance with him in Constantinople, but I can affirm that I am completely unaware whether he took any part in the manufacture of Turkish paper money in question. As for myself, he certainly addressed no word to me on this subject, neither verbally nor in writing. I am entirely unaware whether he had agreements with other accomplices or whether he provided funds for that purpose. It is not true that upon his arrival Calvocoressi delivered to me any letter from Brescianini. It is true that I am informed by public rumor that in Constantinople he had been prosecuted as accused of manufacturing counterfeit Ottoman paper money, but that he had nevertheless been released without any conviction. In short, I know neither from personal knowledge nor from the report of others that he could in any way be involved either in the association of which I was part or in any other.

Further encouragements obtained no additional response, except that he observed that, to his knowledge, Brescianini sent a bill of exchange of 500 francs, though he does not know by what means, but that he, the detainee, believed that this money had been sent to Calvocoressi by Brescianini, who would have been his agent.

After reading and confirmation, he signed the original together with the office.

Alexandre Venanzi

Après les interrogations relatives qui m’ont été faites par cet office, je dirai que l’individu qui m’a été nommé, Avocat Marcel Brescianini, natif de la province de Brescia, et demeurant à Constantinople, où il a une clientèle étendue et est assez connu, est aussi impliqué dans la fabrication des billets turcs en question.

J’ai connu cet individu d’abord à Milan, en 1848 puis à Bologne, et enfin je le revis à Constantinople, mais directement il ne m’adressa aucune parole relative à la fabrication des billets turcs, et la complicité dans ce fait ne m’est connue que par le
rapport de Calvocoressi qui se plaignait que tandis qu’il était à Turin où il devait dépenser d’énormes sommes d’argent, l’avocat Brescianini qui avait pris l’obligation de faire face aux dépenses par moitié s’était borné à lui envoyer seulement cinq cents francs, je ne sais si c’est une lettre de change ou comptant.

J’eus aussi, plus tard, occasion d’entendre parler de Brescianini comme complice, non seulement par le dit Calvocoressi, mais aussi par Venanzi et Spadafora, et Spadafora même ne voulait absolument rien savoir de Brescianini parce qu’il le disait homme compromettant, attendu qu’il avait été déjà accusé à Constantinople pour des crimes semblables, avait déjà subi une perquisition, et n’avait pas eu besoin de peu de fatigues pour sortir d’embarras, en somme je ne puis, le moins du monde, douter que Brescianini soit impliqué dans cette affaire, et par moitié il devait concourir aux dépenses et il avait des rapports avec les complices.

C’est un fait positif pourtant que le dit Brescianini n’a jamais mis les pieds à Turin.

Et cela est ce qu’il me convient de dire comme supplément à mes interrogatoires, et il ne me reste rien d'autre à déclarer n’étant pas informé d’autres faits.

Après lecture et confirmation il a, de même que l’office, signé à l’original. Louis Varallo-Pandolfini.

Translation

Record of the Statements of Louis Varallo-Pandolfini

Today, March 24, 1858, in Turin, in the criminal prisons.

Before us, Charles Druetti, lawyer, judge acting as examining magistrate, with the assistance of the undersigned deputy secretary.

Following the numerous questions already put to Varallo concerning the crime in question, he made the following statement:

The last time I saw Spadafora and Mayner was indeed between two and three o’clock on the 14th of last month, at which time they left the room where the manufacture of Turkish banknotes—about which I have just been questioned—was taking place.

Before their departure, I saw them prepare a bundle using a handkerchief, in which they placed several objects, and more particularly a quantity of Turkish banknotes already completed and bearing stamps and seals, along with two wet seals, and I also believe it likely that they included the oval dry seal, which they had made from very thin copper plates according to the galvanoplastic process. They took everything with them when they left, saying that they would not return until they had received news from Venanzi, who, as I have already stated, was being sought by the police (Questura).

Even before all these events, Venanzi, Mayner, and Spadafora had intended to leave these Royal States in order to continue the manufacture elsewhere; they had even indicated to me the specific locations where they intended to carry out this plan. However, I do not know whether, when Mayner and Spadafora fled from these Royal States, they actually went to those places. They had already withdrawn their passports from the Roman Legation; at least with regard to Spadafora, I am certain that he had obtained his from that Legation; as for the other, I am not certain.

After the relevant interrogations put to me by this office, I state that the individual named to me, the lawyer Marcel Brescianini, a native of the province of Brescia and residing in Constantinople, where he has an extensive clientele and is fairly well known, is also implicated in the manufacture of the Turkish banknotes in question.

I first became acquainted with this individual in Milan in 1848, then in Bologna, and later saw him again in Constantinople; however, he never directly addressed to me any words relating to the manufacture of Turkish banknotes, and his complicity in this matter is known to me only through the report of Calvocoressi, who complained that while he himself was in Turin, where he had to spend enormous sums of money, the lawyer Brescianini—who had undertaken to cover half of the expenses—had limited himself to sending him only five hundred francs; I do not know whether this was by bill of exchange or in cash.

I also later had occasion to hear Brescianini spoken of as an accomplice, not only by the said Calvocoressi but also by Venanzi and Spadafora; and Spadafora himself did not wish to have anything to do with Brescianini, considering him a compromising individual, since he had already been accused in Constantinople of similar crimes, had undergone a search, and had not escaped difficulties without effort. In short, I cannot in the least doubt that Brescianini is involved in this affair; he was to contribute half of the expenses and had relations with the accomplices.

It is, however, a positive fact that the said Brescianini has never set foot in Turin.

This is all that I deem appropriate to add to my interrogations, and I have nothing further to declare, not being informed of any other facts.

After reading and confirmation, he signed the original together with the office.

Louis Varallo-Pandolfini
